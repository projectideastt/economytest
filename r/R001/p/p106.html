Effective management strategies for wastewater treatment, including reuse are limited. Moreover, a lack of technical capabilities to operate and maintain the existing facilities for sewage treatment makes it difficult to control wastewater. The septic tank has been an effective treatment method for wastewater in areas occupied by a small population. A number of problems, however, have been identified, such as limited control over design, lack of sludge removal and poor facilities to dispose of sludge. Its extensive use in heavy populated areas makes this a serious challenge. 4.5.2 Pressures Pressures on land and water bodies are as a consequence of ineffective methods of solid and liquid waste disposal. These include burning of refuse and dumping into rivers and swamps, indiscriminate use of agricultural chemicals, absence of appropriate disposal methods and facilities for hazardous waste.

Expanding urbanisation has concentrated a growing proportion of the population into high rise structures and dense subdivisions, and this leads to an increase in solid and liquid waste. However, only a fraction of industrial and municipal wastewater is treated before being discharged into the surrounding land and water bodies. WASA indicated that 30% of the population is connected to sewerage systems while septic tank and pit latrines account for 58 and 10% respectively. Two percent of the population has no known means of sewage disposal (Grimes 2005). More than 40% of the national population lives in the East-West Corridor alone. There is thus a shortfall in sewerage services to residents in the urban areas. In 2002 solid waste disposal at the three municipal disposal sites accounted for approximately 427 tonnes (or 33%) of the 1300 tonnes of projected waste generated. There were no data on the quantities disposed in the regional corporation facilities or the THA site. However, the quantum of garbage actually reaching the dump sites was estimated at 85% in 1995 by SWMCOL. There is a plethora of unofficial and illegal dump sites throughout the country, some 300 or so as identified by SWMCOL. In the rural areas, they are to be found at abandoned estate traces on former Caroni lands, in accessible areas of forest reserves, in swamps and along river courses. In the urban centres and particularly in residential areas with difficult terrain, where streets are narrow and sometimes access to homes are by steps, the dumps can be seen at several of the intersections of steps and streets, in uncovered drains and vacant and unattended lots.
