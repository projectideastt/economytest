Chapter 8: Economy, Industry, Resource Use

8.1 Overview The natural resources of Trinidad and Tobago have been and continue to be the foundation for socio-economic development. The country was principally an agricultural economy up to the beginning of the twentieth century, when economic development was based mainly on sugar production, and smaller outputs of cocoa, coconuts, and coffee. Today, however, the exploitation of oil and natural gas resources drives the economy. Now on its second energy boom, Trinidad and Tobago has developed several energy intensive industries, including a range of petrochemicals, thus achieving the rank of being amongst the top five LNG producers in the world. The country is the largest exporter of ammonia and methanol, and is the third largest nitrogen producer globally. The world’s largest methanol plant can be found in Trinidad and Tobago. Undoubtedly, the per capita use and total use of natural gas have increased significantly. Apart from the demand for natural gas which is used as a fuel and a feedstock, petrochemical industries create a demand for power and water. Notwithstanding the contribution of the oil and gas sector to the economy, the utilisation of other natural resources has also contributed to recent socio-economic development in Trinidad and Tobago. Coastal and marine resources are important for both the fisheries and tourism industries, particularly to the economy of Tobago where both are the mainstay. Although Tobago’s main tourism product is based primarily on sun, sea, sand and reefs, it has a growing eco-tourism industry based upon the natural resources of Little Tobago and the Main Ridge, the oldest forest reserve in the western hemisphere, and the peaceful lifestyle of neighbouring villages such as Speyside and Charlotteville. For Tobago, the processing of flying fish is an important economic activity, with the operation of three fish processing plants.
