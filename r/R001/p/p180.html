The issue of quarrying has been dealt with as part of Chapter Four. 8.2 Energy Use 8.2.1 Description The energy sector is the impetus for growth in the Trinidad and Tobago economy accounting for 40.5 % of total GDP in 2004 and 41.2 % in 2005 with an overall projected growth of 20.6 % in the sector. The petrochemical sub-sector, which includes the production of methanol, nitrogenous fertilisers (ammonia and urea), and natural gas liquids, continues to expand along with the increased production of natural gas (Ministry of Finance 2006). This expansion in the energy sector is propelled mainly by the commencement of operations at the BHP Billiton oil field in early 2005 and the coming on stream of the Atlantic LNG Train IV in December 2005. This new train increased Atlantic LNG's overall output by almost 50 %, making this the largest LNG train in the world at 5.2 million tonnes per year of LNG. As a result, Trinidad and Tobago is now positioned as the fifth-largest exporter of LNG worldwide (Ministry of Finance 2006). 8.2.2 Pressure The Vision 2020 National Development Strategy calls for an expansion of the energy sector into value-added oil and gas-based products and services along the value chain (Ministry of Planning and Development 2006). Therefore, the country’s economic future remains entrenched in the energy sector as further efforts are made to deepen its gas-based industrialisation process. Consequently, in 2005, 2 additional plants were commissioned, bringing the total number of gas-based plants in the country to 27 (Table 8.1). This number is expected to further increase with the emergence of new prospects for additional downstream gas-based activities. The construction of 2 aluminium smelter plants is proposed within the next five years (Box 8.1), along with plans for the establishment of plants for production of melamine, urea, ammonia nitrate, acetic acid and polypropylene51. This growing industrial capacity and booming economic activities have implications for the country’s oil and gas reserves, water resources as well as for electricity generation. Already some experts are questioning whether the country has sufficient energy reserves to sustain these industries in the long term. In 2004, oil and gas reserves stood at 756 million proven barrels of oil and 18.8 trillion cubic feet of proven gas reserves, respectively (Table 8.2). With respect to the natural gas exports, the USA remains the main market for Trinidad and Tobago LNG, with between 75 and 80 % of all LNG imported into the USA originating from Trinidad and Tobago.
