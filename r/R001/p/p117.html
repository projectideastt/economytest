The nation’s reliance on oil and gas to fuel its economic development thrust is expected to grow as more companies set up operations on the west coast of Trinidad. Approximately five new gas-based companies are expected to be developed over the next three to five years to add to the 27 already in operation. This is elaborated in Chapter Eight. 5.2.4 Response The Government enacted a number of laws to protect the marine environment and acceded to a number of international conventions which focus on the marine environment. The relevant local legislation includes the Shipping (Marine Pollution) Bill, No. 2, 2004 (this bill lapsed in September, 2005), the Marine Areas (Preservation and Enhancement) Act Chapter 37:02, (Act No. 1 of 1970, amended in 1996) and the Territorial Sea Act, Chapter 1:51 (Act No. 38 of 1969, amended in 1986). International conventions include the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), Convention for the Protection and Development of the Marine Environment of the Wider Caribbean Region, and the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL). The Water Pollution Rules, once implemented, are expected to introduce a stricter measure of control on effluent discharge into the environment. It is useful to note that the rules will limit effluent discharge at the point of discharge only, with no control over cumulative limits in the marine environment. In furtherance of protection of its coasts and inshore waters, the Government has updated its National Oil Spill Contingency Plan (developed in 1997 and revised in 2004) and established a bilateral Oil Spill Contingency Plan with Venezuela (in 1989). 5.3 Marine Transportation 5.3.1 Description Marine transportation in the nation’s waters includes the movement of goods and passengers, both of which are increasing. Figure 5.4 highlights the main shipping routes in the Gulf of Paria. It is estimated that there are 10,000 commercial vessels moving through Trinidad and Tobago's waters annually13. Ships from 12 shipping lines currently call at the Port of Point Lisas and they originate mostly from Europe (Rotterdam, Antwerp and Le Havre) and North America (Houston, Miami, New Orleans) 14. Special marine goods vessels such as oil and liquefied gas tankers, cement barges and container ships, move to and from the various ports on the west coast of Trinidad and through the Gulf of Paria on a daily basis. Information was not readily available on ship movements by ports and quantities of goods being imported and exported monthly or annually.
