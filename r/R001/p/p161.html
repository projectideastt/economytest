Today, the parks and protected areas consist of 12 areas of which 4 are natural areas comprising some 6,904 ha. The management objectives for these areas are to protect their ecosystems and natural features and to provide opportunities for environmental education, research, and interpretation and some forms of recreation. Area Reforested The regeneration of degraded forest ecosystems is achieved through the reforestation efforts of public sector entities. Prominent among the public sector initiatives is the co-management programme, the NRWRP, which stands out because of its mandate to replant 13,367 ha of degraded forest lands over a 10-year period (Box 2). Its Status Report for 2004/2006 documents the achievements of the programme Box 2: The NRWRP (Source: NRWRP 2006)

In the past, attempts at community-based forest protection and conservation projects by both public and private institutions have been small and localised. However in 2003 the large scale, long term, community-based National Reforestation and Watershed Management Programme began to provide a number of ecological and social benefits including “an increase in the value of forest capital in Trinidad and Tobago, preservation of biodiversity, enhanced watersheds, increasing aquifer discharge, flood avoidance, increase food production through agro-forestry, environmental and aesthetic enhancement and the alleviation of poverty through sustainable employment particularly among women and youth.” One of the principal targets of this co-management Programme is the replanting of 13,367 ha of state forest lands and reserves over a 10-year period, including the replanting of 4,452 ha of watershed which has been denuded or destroyed. The Programme calls for the initial replanting of 2,440 ha with 1,214 ha replanted annually thereafter with the anticipated involvement of 80 communities generating employment for 2,500 persons. It consisted of two main components namely the planting component geared at replanting forested areas, and maintenance component designed to strengthen the capacity for policing forested areas. The major programme activities included:

 Identification of and defining denuded forested lands targeted for action;  Implementation of annual plan of action by community groups for their given area;  Preparation of the targeted denuded lands for planting;  Production and planting of seedlings;  Maintenance and protecting restored lands from fires and other hazards;  Implementation and improvement of watershed management practices;  Public education and awareness; and  Facilitating and supporting forest and non-forest management entrepreneurial initiatives within the communities.

Three years later, some 53 community-based organisations under the Programme had employed 1,855 persons and had replanted 1,825 ha, constructed 4,000 check dams, maintained 7,135 ha of forests, and protected 14,042 ha of forests. In addition, some groups opened and maintained 238 ha of parks and 271 km of trails, and cleared and cleaned 38 km of river courses of debris. Working with communities meant that the Programme can tap into indigenous knowledge about flora and fauna species in planning and implementing the forest restoration work plans and at the same time provide the necessary training in forest management and conservation to shift negative practices and behaviour relating to their interactions with the forests. To this end, 11,845 persons have been trained under this Programme on a variety of topics relating to forest management and protection and small business management.

In Trinidad, the Programme covers three zones, engaging 19 groups each in the North and the South, and 14 in the East. In Tobago the Programme, which falls under the control of the THA, currently works with three communities.
