Externally-propelled economic forces, labour shortages and local pests plagued the agricultural sector. Modifications to crops cultivated ensued with sugar cane cultivation giving way in some areas to coconuts, the regeneration of cocoa and coffee, and the cultivation of a wider range of local food crops including fruits and vegetables. Thus, several of these crops found niches, as different agricultural crops were cultivated in different parts of the islands with varying degrees of success. Sugar cane, coffee, and cocoa organised on the basis of large plantations and occupying different geographic locations emerged as the dominant export crops by the middle of the 20th century. The north-eastern, eastern and southern coasts of Trinidad and south-west Tobago produced coconuts, while the valleys and slopes of the Central and Northern Ranges of Trinidad and the slopes of the Main Ridge of Tobago produced cocoa. The Caroni, Nariva and Naparima Plains in Trinidad produced sugar cane. Local food crops including pulses (legumes), vegetables, root crops and rice were historically grown by the providers of labour principally on lands marginal to export crop production. However, in Tobago, cultivation of food crops occupied less marginal, if not prime, land as a developed peasantry acquired and utilised plantation land for food production. Today, the production of sugar cane, the country’s major export crop, has declined considerably, while vegetable production for domestic consumption has become a viable industry. Agriculture’s share to the nation’s GDP declined over the years, moving from 1.42% in 2000 to 0.80% in 2004, with minor increases in 2001 and 2002. According to the 2004 Agricultural Census (CSO 2005c), there were 18,022 agricultural holdings in Trinidad and 946 in Tobago, and they contained parcel sizes from as small as less than 0.5 ha to as large as over 500 ha. In Tobago, the single largest number of holdings was in the category less than 0.5 ha, which accounted for approximately 46% of all holdings. In Trinidad, the single largest number of holdings was in the category 2 ha to less than 5 ha, which accounted for approximately 35% of all holdings. Holdings of less than 2 ha accounted for approximately 52% of all parcels in Trinidad and about 76% in Tobago. Thus the majority of holdings are small. The 26,131 total agricultural parcels extend over a land mass of 84,990 ha of land of which Trinidad accounted for 24,856 parcels on 82,948 ha. Tobago accounted for 1,275 parcels on 2,042 ha. The uses to which these parcels are put were identified in the 2004 Agricultural Census under nine categories as indicated in the Table 4.12.
