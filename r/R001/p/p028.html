Chapter 1: New Approach to Reporting on the State of the Environment

1.1 Purpose of Reporting In the face of continued degradation of the natural environment, the Government of the Republic of Trinidad and Tobago (GORTT) long ago recognised the need for a single agency to take charge of management of the environment. Its job was to deal with the multiplicity of disparate pieces of legislation relating to different aspects of the environment and administered by a plethora of government agencies engaged in its management and protection. However, it was not until 1995 that the Government enacted the Environmental Management Act (Act 3 of 1995) which provided a framework for engaging natural resource exploitation through environmentally sensitive approaches and created the legal, financial, and institutional mechanisms to guide and oversee the development of the environment. Five years later, this Act was repealed and replaced by the Environmental Management Act (Act 3 of 2000) (the EM Act). Of the many vehicles identified in the EM Act to effect policy direction, coordination, environmental controls, information dissemination, reporting and compliance, one was the preparation and submission of the Annual Report. The Assessment of the State of the Environment (ASOE) serves several purposes. First, as previously mentioned, it is a requirement of the EM Act (Section 14[1]), and the Environmental Management Authority (EMA) is therefore mandated to produce an annual report each year, a segment of which is devoted to an assessment of the state of the Trinidad and Tobago’s environment. Secondly, it provides an opportunity to measure environmental performance and critically examine the pressures being placed, through actions or lack thereof, on various components of the environment. The ASOE therefore offers a focal point for the systematic acquisition and application of scientific knowledge to the national development process and, through public education, informs the public of the state of the environment and provides guidance to all stakeholders in the pursuit of sustainable development. Finally, it meets the requirements of the GORTT’s international obligations to agreements such as the United Nations Convention on Biological Diversity (UNCBD) which emerged from the 1992 United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (the Earth Summit). 1.2 Past Approach to Reporting With two exceptions, past reports on the state of the environment dealt primarily with a single thematic environmental issue. The EMA’s 1996 State of the Environment Report assessed the nation’s Biodiversity (EMA 1998a), and this was followed by Freshwater (EMA 1998b), Air Pollution (EMA 2001a), and Noise Pollution (EMA 2004). For the years 2001 and 2002, a biennial report (2001/2002) was prepared focusing on an Environmental Vulnerability Index (EVI) developed for Trinidad and Tobago (Agard and Gowrie 2003). The 2004 State of the Environment Report (Northern Range Assessment 2005) was the most marked departure from the traditional reporting approach. Drawing on an assessment of the Northern Range which was conducted as part of a global process known as the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA), this report focused on the Northern Range as an integrated ecosystem, thereby allowing for an examination of the cross-sectoral approach that is required for effective management. The process of preparing this report also focused on participation of all relevant stakeholders and users.
