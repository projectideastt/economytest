Tobago, on the other hand, is approximately 42 km long from north-east to south-west and 12 km from north to south and consists of two main physiographic regions, namely: the Main Ridge in the northeast, and the Coastal Plain in the southwest. The island’s highest point, some 650 m above sea level, is located on the Main Ridge which runs for about two thirds of the length of the island in a south-west to north-east orientation. Utilisation of the nation’s land resources is organised primarily around: agriculture–including forestry; mining; urbanisation; waste disposal; and transportation. In 2004, agriculture, excluding forestry, accounted for some 85,000 ha while it was estimated that forestry covered some 48% of the nation’s land or approximately 248,000 ha. Thus agriculture and forestry combined accounted for 333,000 ha. Mining accounted for a substantial but indeterminate quantity of land. Approximately 2,833 ha were under license to quarry operators. Data were not available on land licensed to companies engaged in land-based oil and gas mining. In the case of urbanisation, it is estimated that some 15% or 127,000 ha of the land mass is urbanised. However, data are not readily available on the size of the urban centres even though the sizes of the individual cities and boroughs are known. The contiguous areas which are separated administratively from the city and borough centres are in some instances intensely urban, but have not been recently reflected in the available data. This chapter deals with the four principal areas of land use and expects to build over time on the data that are not readily available. 4.2 Urbanisation 4.2.1 Description The latter half of the 20th century saw the expansion of some of the traditional towns and villages; the densification of old uses; and the development of housing estates, resort facilities and commercial and industrial sites on green field sites throughout the nation.
