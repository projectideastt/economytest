(Source: Meteorological Services Division; pers. comm. 2006)

Flooding Since 1989, flooding has become a perennial problem in many communities, particularly in Trinidad where heavy rains cause flooding along the floodplains of major rivers, often resulting in damage to home and property, and even in the loss of lives, including the lives of children who are caught off guard by the rising, raging waters. Over the years, the incidence of flooding has increased significantly and financial claims have been substantial. In Trinidad and Tobago, flood damage was estimated at US$2.4 million in 2000 and US$8 million in 20022. The Meteorological Services Division collects information on the incidence of flooding in Trinidad and Tobago. From data collected from 1981 to 2005 it was revealed that the Caroni, Caparo and North Oropouche River Basins are notable with regard to the incidence of persistent flooding, with the occurrence of two or more flooding events per year. In recent times the most severely affected areas have been areas along the Caroni River, with almost twice as many flood events than the Caparo and North Oropouche Rivers (Figure 3.10). Moreover, the problem no longer seems to be restricted to the traditional October to December period. Instead flooding now extends from June to January, and gets an early start when there is above average rainfall during the dry season. Figure 3.11 shows the distribution of flood events between 1981 and January, 2005.
