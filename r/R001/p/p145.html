6.4.3 Current State and Trends Wetlands are in varying stages of degradation and, until recently, were essentially left unmanaged and minimally resourced. There is little history of wetlands protection or management to the extent afforded to protected areas such as forest reserves and game sanctuaries. Thus wetlands have largely suffered from neglect; although some portions within the Caroni and Nariva Swamps were provided legal protection when prohibited areas like the game sanctuaries and forest reserves were located within their boundaries. 6.4.4 Response To some extent, the Marine Areas (Preservation and Enhancement) Act; Act 1 of 1970 brought protection for the wetlands under its umbrella. Here the Act did not name coastal wetlands but described them as “… submarine areas within the Territorial Seas and includes any adjoining land or swamp areas which form with certain submarine areas a single ecological entity” (National Wetlands Committee 2002). More than two decades later (in 1992), the Government became a party to the Convention on Wetlands and signalled its intention to protect and restore wetlands in Trinidad and Tobago for the use of current and future generations. In 1995 a National Wetlands Committee was established and tasked with the preparation of a national wetlands policy. A National Wetlands Plan was prepared and eventually was accepted by the Government in 2001 (National Wetlands Committee 2002). The Plan identifies six policies which are expected to guide wetlands protection and rehabilitation and they are noted as follows: The Government of Trinidad and Tobago will: 1. promote awareness and understanding of the wetland resources in Trinidad and Tobago and actively encourage participation of landowners, NGOs and institutions in wetland conservation; 2. develop exemplary practices in support of wetland conservation and sustainable wetland use when it designs and implements government programmes on publicly owned land and waters in keeping with the wise use principle as defined under the Convention on Wetlands; 3. preserve outstanding examples of all wetland types in Trinidad and Tobago by including them in a system of national parks and other protected natural areas; 4. be an active partner in cooperative activities, which promote wetland conservation in Trinidad and Tobago and the Caribbean region; 5. support and promote development of the scientific and technological expertise needed for wetland conservation and ensure that this expertise is accessible to planners, managers, regulators and other decision-makers; 6. identify weaknesses in the present institutional and organisational structures relevant to wetland conservation and will develop activities for their improvement. To date some three sites have been designated Ramsar Sites. Nariva Swamp which was illegally used for mechanised rice production is currently being brought back to health after the removal of the rice farmers and others. Agencies with direct responsibility for oversight for environmental matters relating to wetland management are Forestry Division; the Land and Water Development Division in the MALMR; the IMA; and the EMA.
