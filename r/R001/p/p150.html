The incidence of annual forest fires, which impacts negatively on wildlife communities and ecological processes, represents a major avenue for forest loss and has tested the capacity of the Forestry Division to effectively protect and manage the country’s forest resources. Figure 7.2 shows the number of forest fires and the area burnt in Trinidad over a 17 year period 1987 to 2003 (Singh 2003). Every year, there is an average of 315 recorded forest fires in Trinidad burning an average of 4,083 ha, with a substantial number of these fires occurring in degraded forest lands where fire-climax grassland vegetation have been established after these persistent annual fires. This trend of above average numbers of fires and acreage burnt was sustained in 6 of the 17 years reviewed, of which 4 of them were El Niño years. A disturbing feature of these above average years is that a relatively high amount of damage is sustained in the natural forests. Most of the fires are maliciously set. Though recent focus has been paid to community-based fire prevention strategies, Forestry Division cites public indifference to the values of forests and ignorance and disregard for the negative impacts of forest fires as key reasons for the persistence of this annual problem. In Tobago, the number of fires averaged 200 per year between 2000 and 2004, with few fires occurring in Northeast Tobago where the Main Ridge is located39. Instead these annual fires mainly occur on the abandoned agricultural estates in the St David and St Patrick Parishes. No data were received on the amount of acreages burnt in Tobago. Decreasing River Water Quantity and Quality Over the years, human activities have impacted on riverine water quantity and quality, changing the habitat and the floral and faunal characteristics of rivers, particularly in urban and built up areas. Generally, river water flow and levels have been altered considerably with changes in vegetative pattern particularly in the upper watershed region. River water flow in the dry season is down to a trickle or virtually non-existent in some rivers where flow was observed previously during this time. Recent work conducted by WASA revealed that water flow in some rivers in the East West Corridor during the dry season is due mainly to discharges from neighbouring treatment plants. The quality of river water has declined considerably in many areas particularly in highly populated areas, as evident by high levels of five-day biological oxygen demand (BOD5), bacterial content and turbidity, and by the presence of chemical pollutants. This is due mainly to effluent discharges from industrial sources; inappropriate flood control measures; indiscriminate dumping of solid waste in water courses, including the dumping of construction waste; and the presence of high sediment loads in the runoff particularly from hillsides and upstream locations. Pollution of riverine water affects the ability of rivers to provide productive habitats for terrestrial and aquatic species and may have direct harmful or deadly effects on organisms. In-stream problems due to pollution are further exacerbated during periods of low flows when the dilution effect is at its lowest. In studies conducted by researchers from the UWI it was found that some freshwater fish and shrimp migrate during their life cycles. Thus, pollution at any point within the river can affect them. The studies show where mountain mullet or freshwater shrimp are no longer found in upstream tributaries of the Caroni River as in the past, since their natural migrations are effectively ‘blocked’ by the polluted lower river (Alkins-Koo 2005).
