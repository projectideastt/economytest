The main GHGs, which occur naturally in the atmosphere, are water vapour, carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. Increases in these gases enhance the atmosphere’s capacity to trap heat energy which leads to increases in the Earth’s surface temperature. The processes and impact of increased GHG is known as the enhanced greenhouse effect. Since the industrial era human-induced increases in the level of GHGs, particularly carbon dioxide, are reportedly responsible for changes in today’s global and regional climatic patterns. In the absence of on-going research, at this stage one could only deduce what adverse impacts of climate change would likely be on Trinidad and Tobago. The negative climate change impacts due to increased GHG emissions that may affect Trinidad and Tobago, some of which are examined extensively in the Initial National Communication for the UNFCCC (EMA 2001b), include: • Sea level rise and extreme rainfall events which will serve to exacerbate the problem of coastal erosion, damage to coastal infrastructure, and perennial flooding in low lying coastal areas. Many coastal communities are susceptible to coastal erosion particularly communities along the east, southeast, northwest and southwest coasts of Trinidad, and southwest Tobago. The IMA noted a 1 m per year and higher loss of coastline in the Ortoire region in the 1990s, while Singh (1997) reported on an approximate 2 m loss of coastline in Coral Point and Icacos, a 2.5 m loss in Guayaguayare, and 3 m annual loss in Los Iros. However, accretion was observed close to Punta de Arenal at a rate of 2.3 m annually  Rise in air temperature which increases the risk of crop damage, reduces agricultural production and negatively impacts the livelihoods of domestic and export farmers and their families
