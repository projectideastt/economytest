A study was conducted of paediatric asthma patients who attended the Accident and Emergency (A&E) department of the Paediatric Priority Care Facility at the Eric Williams Medical Sciences Complex in relation to Saharan dust visibility and other climatic variables for the period 23 May, 2001 to 13 May, 2002. During the study period, some 2,655 patients visited A&E department with acute asthma and the results established a link between increased paediatrics asthma admissions and increased Saharan dust cover. Apart from the link to respiratory problems in humans, it has been discovered that the toxic fungal pathogen known as Aspergillus sydowii was travelling in the dust particles and could be responsible for sea fan diseases in the Caribbean.

3.6.4 Response The response of the Sahara dust challenge has mainly been from a research perspective, looking at the cause and effect relationship between the emergency admissions of children with acute asthma and the occurrence of Sahara dust episodes. This work is currently being undertaken in collaboration with the US Geological Survey (USGS). A station, supplied by the USGS, has been established at the top of a lighthouse on Galera Point at the northeast tip of Trinidad, where air coming from Africa is sampled before it moves inland. This station provides information on the concentrations of airborne dust, metals, persistent organic pollutants, and microorganisms coming across the Atlantic. The results provide background information which can be compared with air-quality data from the industrialised areas of Trinidad. It is hoped that the effect of particulates, persistent organic pollutants (POPs), metals, and microbes coming from Africa can be separated from the effect of locally generated pollutants on emergency admissions for asthma. These data will also be compared with similar data from Cape Verde, a group of islands off Africa’s west coast.

On July 24, 2003, a Saharan dust storm was draped over the Atlantic Ocean and the Canary Islands off northwest Africa. Source: NASA. 2003.
