Trinidad and Tobago is a very mobile society with a significant proportion of the population traveling outside of their communities to key urban centres to access jobs, schools and government services. Most travel within the islands involves private cars and taxis (cars, small and large maxi-taxis), while movement between Trinidad and Tobago is achieved via air and sea travel. The number of vehicles on the nation’s roads has increased dramatically. Between 1960 and 2000 the level of motorisation increased almost four-fold from 63 registered vehicles per 1,000 persons in 1960, to 250 vehicles per 1000 persons in 2000. By 2005, the number of vehicles per 1,000 persons had increased further to 312. The current rates of vehicle ownership and traffic growth have placed great demands on the national transportation infrastructure resulting in gridlock in many of the country’s main roadways and access roads. In response the State continues to expand and upgrade the road infrastructure. A comprehensive National Transportation Plan was commissioned in 2004 which attempts to address the long term sea, air and land transportation needs of the country. There are also plans for a light rail mass transit system. CONCLUSION Reporting on the state of the environment arose out of the recognition internationally that such systems are required as part of the sustainable system of managing and conserving our natural resources which are increasingly under threat. This 2005 report represents a new system for reporting on the state of the Trinidad and Tobago environment. It provides a more comprehensive outlook of the condition and trends of our natural resources, the pressures that affect these resources and society’s responses to our environmental challenges. The report provides information in a form that we hope makes it valuable to decision makers, policy makers, planners and the general public alike.
