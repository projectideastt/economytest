Role of Co-Management in Species Protection The NGO and community sector have been instrumental in raising awareness of the value of biodiversity in Trinidad and Tobago and the benefits of conservation and protection of natural resources. A number of NGOs and community based organisations (CBOs) in north-east and south-east Trinidad and in Tobago have worked with State agencies in managing the country’s biodiversity through the conduct of community-based conservation, eco-tourism, and research projects. 7.4 Biodiversity and Climate Change The biodiversity of Trinidad and Tobago, like elsewhere, is largely characterised by its climate regime among other things. Any changes in climate therefore are likely to directly and indirectly affect biodiversity. Increased ambient and sea temperatures; changing rainfall patterns and amounts; rising sea level; and increased frequency of upset climatic events (such as storms and hurricanes) would adversely alter the country’s ecosystems and affect species habitat and food supply. 7.4.1 Pressure The levels of carbon dioxide in the earth’s atmosphere have been on the increase since the coming of the industrial age. Along with this increase in carbon dioxide levels are changes in the global climate. These predicted changes in global climate and changes in the climate of Trinidad and Tobago are discussed in some detail in Chapter Three and summarised in Table 7.14. Global climate has changed significantly in the last 150 years. Currently, temperature increase is accelerating and this will have direct and indirect, regional and local impacts, with some of the most severe impacts predicted to take place on SIDS (IPCC 2001b). A temperature increase as high as 6 ºC (worst case scenario) is predicted by 2100, with increasingly severe storms in the Caribbean. It is difficult to predict the changes in rainfall that are to be expected since the confidence level is low. A report done on behalf of the Department for International Development (DFID) indicates that latest projections suggest that rainfall is more likely to be higher in the Eastern Caribbean during December to February, and more likely to be lower between June-August. Already there is some evidence of increased rainfall during the early dry season period (December – January) but it is too early to suggest that this will be a future trend. Moreover, changes in climate elsewhere could affect the Caribbean region. For example, the increased occurrence of drought in northern Africa is believed to be responsible for the increased frequency of Sahara dust events noted in the Caribbean. 7.4.2 Current State and Trends A number of potential impacts on species habitat and biodiversity have been identified with changing climate and sea level rise. Among the most pronounced and relevant in Trinidad and Tobago are:  Terrestrial ecosystems species – The survival and distribution of most species are influenced by climate. Many species have been able to successfully respond to climate change throughout evolutionary time; today the main concern is the impact this accelerated rate of change will have on species. The 2001 IPCC Report (IPCC 2001b) notes that impoverishment of species has occurred in the past during times of abrupt or rapid climate change. Impacts of climate change on species would also likely be from physiological stress and changes and loss in habitat, especially from fires and increased storm events.
