According to the 2003 Annual Report of the Forestry Division (Forestry Division 2004), the 36 forest reserves in Trinidad and Tobago occupy 131,432 ha of proclaimed forest reserve, and an additional 11,652 ha of un-proclaimed forest reserves (Table 7.5), thus representing 27.9 % the country’s total land area. Some of these forested lands are severely degraded as a result of years of fires, illegal logging, quarrying, residential and agricultural squatting, as well as the practice of slash and burn land clearance. There are some 56,000 ha of private forests which also serve the function of biodiversity conservation. The 13 wildlife sanctuaries declared under the Conservation of Wildlife Act (67:01), occupying 17,076 ha in 2003, serve as safe havens for certain threatened and vulnerable species as well as the protection of their habitats and food supply. It has been proposed to designate the Matura National Park as an ESA, (Figure 7.4). Matura has a good representation of Mora forests and is also the home to several species that appear in the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), including orchids, the ocelot (Leopardus pardalis), the tayra (Eira barbara), the red howler monkey (Alouatta seniculus), the wild hog/quenk (Tayassu tajacu), the matte (Tupinambis teguixin), the pawi (Pipile pipile) and cage bird species such as bullfinch and picoplat.
