3.5.3 Current State and Trends Trinidad and Tobago imports all of its requirements for ODS. The State of the Environment Report, 2000 (EMA 2001a) noted that consumption data is a good guide in determining emissions of ODS since discharged or leaked ODS in existing installations are replaced by new consumption. In new installations, without recycling, ODS will also be eventually released into the atmosphere, and need to be replaced by new consumption. Figure 3.21 and Table 3.7 show the consumption of CFCs and other ODS in Trinidad and Tobago for the period 1990 to 2000. Consumption of ODS declined up until 1993, after which a steady rise in consumption was observed, reaching a peak of 156 tonnes in 1998. A decrease in consumption was once again recorded in 1999 followed by another increase in ODS consumption. However, the country recorded an ODS consumption of 52.1 ODP tonnes in 2005. 3.5.4 Response One hundred and ninety one countries worldwide have made the initial legal commitment to stop the production and to phase-out the use of all main types of ODS through the Montreal Protocol. Annex 4 summarises the Montreal Protocol Control Measures.
