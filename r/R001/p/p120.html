5.4 Fisheries Resource Exploitation 5.4.1 Description For centuries, the fisheries resources in Trinidad and Tobago’s waters have supported fisher folk. Almost all coastal communities were once fishing villages. This has changed over the years because of a reduction in the number of coastal residents engaged in fishing, increasing hazard due to piracy in the nation’s waters, a decrease in the number of traditional moorings, destruction of some old fishing grounds and theft of boat engines. However, based on 1998 data, the FAO indicated that there were a total of 100 landing sites across the two islands, of which 80 were in Trinidad and 20 in Tobago. However, only about 42 (12 in Tobago and 30 in Trinidad) were equipped with landing areas, storage rooms and other facilities (http://www.fao.org/fi/fep/en/TTO/profile.htm). The nation’s fisher folk are mostly artisanal. However, they are generally an aging group with a significant number of part time workers. In 1998 they owned and/or operated an estimated 1,417 fishing vessels of which 1,216 were registered in Trinidad and 255 registered in Tobago. These fishermen operating fishing vessels which spanned four size categories, namely under 9 metres, 9 to 12 metres, 12 to 15 metres and over 15 metres, are responsible for approximately 80% of the national annual catch (http://www.fao.org/fi/fep/en/TTO/profile.htm). According to the FAO, in 2000 approximately 6,700 people were employed in the fishing industry, including aquaculture but approximately 37% (2,500) were part time fishermen. Additionally, the fisheries sub-sector employed 10% of the labour force engaged in agriculture and contributed exports to the approximate value of TT$14 million (http://www.fao.org/fi/fep/en/TTO/profile.htm). The IMA identified 19 main fish landing sites in Trinidad and three in Tobago and of these, the single largest number of sites were located on the north coast of Trinidad (Table 5.2 and Figure 5.5). It is worth noting that fishing villages like Grand Chemin and Orange Valley in Trinidad are also consistent producers of large catches over the years. Although Tobago has a large number of fishing villages, only three main landing sites were listed as shown in Table 5.2 and Figure 5.5. However, villages like Castara, Charlotteville and Speyside are particularly active fishing villages supporting large fleets and conducting fish sales right on their beaches.
