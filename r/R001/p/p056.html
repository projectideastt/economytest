It is, therefore, understandable that over the years several regional institutions and projects have been established to ensure that this is a reality. Trinidad and Tobago has participated or is participating in the regional project Caribbean Planning for Adaptation to Global Climate Change (CPACC), and the subsequent Mainstreaming Adaptation to Global Climate Change (MACC) project, is field testing a manual on the Clean Development Mechanism, and is developing a National Energy Policy Framework proposed by the Caribbean Renewable Energy Development Programme (CREDP). The country is also part of the Caribbean Community Climate Change Centre (CCCCC) whose role is: a) to protect the climate system of Member countries for the benefit of present and future generations; b) to enhance regional institutional capabilities for the co-ordination of national responses to the negative effects of climate change; c) to provide comprehensive policy and technical support in the area of climate change and related issues and spearheading regional initiatives in those areas; and d) to perform the role of executing agency for regional environmental projects relating to climate change. Behavioural change is seen as key to environmental management and so the EMA has conducted lectures and discussions with communities and schools in an effort to inform about the issues of climate change. With the signs of climate change now validated, sustainable land management is a critical area which needs to be addressed if Trinidad and Tobago is to successfully adapt to its effects. In recent times, the policy framework relating to the sustainable use and management of land resources has been strengthened with the development of the National Action Programme (NAP) to Combat Land Degradation (MPUE 2006), the enunciation of a new Forest Policy for Trinidad and Tobago in 1998 (Forestry Division, 1998), the Strategic Plan for the Forestry Division (Forestry Division 2001), and the adoption of a National Wetlands Policy in 2001 (National Wetlands Committee 2002). The rehabilitation of the Nariva Swamp began in 1996 after Government brought to an end large-scale intensive agricultural activities taking place in the area. Forestry Division has an on-going and active reforestation programme, and several community-based organisations are actively involved in the reforestation of denuded watersheds particularly in the Northern Range. Perhaps one of the significant activities has been the implementation of the community-based National Reforestation and Watershed Rehabilitation Programme (NRWRP) in 2004 which targets the replanting of 33,030 ha of forest over a 10 year period. If successfully implemented this programme has the potential to substantially increase forest cover which serves as sinks for carbon dioxide. Further information on the programme is provided in the chapter on biodiversity (see Chapter Seven). 3.3 Enhanced Greenhouse Effect and Global Warming 3.3.1 Background The average temperature of the Earth is kept at about 15 ºC by the blanket of atmosphere that surrounds it. Without this atmosphere, the average temperature would be about minus 18 ºC which would be too cold to successfully sustain life. Achieving this average temperature is possible due to the ‘natural greenhouse effect’. The Sun’s energy travels to the Earth, most of which passes through the atmosphere. This energy is absorbed by the Earth’s surface, warming it and increasing its temperature, and in turn, heat energy is radiated from the Earth into the atmosphere. This heat energy is absorbed by certain gases in the atmosphere, thus trapping this energy there and increasing the temperature and maintaining the Earth’s temperature and energy balance.
