Game Species The Wildlife Section of the Forestry Division prepares an annual report which provides information on the annual sale of game licences, annual number of hunters, and the annual harvest data of wild meat. These data can be used to monitor and assess legal hunting. However illegal hunting, which refers to hunting without a licence and outside of the hunting season, is a major issue and as such alternative methods must be used to evaluate the extent of the problem. Game animals are hunted legally for five months, between October and February. The number of animal species exploited continues to be small compared to the total number of animal species found in Trinidad and Tobago. Game licences were issued for agouti, cage birds, deer, lappe, alligator, iguana, quenk (wildhog), armadillo (tattoo) and waterfowl. Over the last 16 years the numbers of hunters and State game licences issued have increased steadily (Figures 7.7 and 7.8). In the 1990/91 season the number of licensed hunters totaled 5,880 but this figure has increased by over 50 % reaching 9,133 licensed hunters in the 2004/05 hunting season. Similarly, some 7,428 game licences were issued for the 1990/91 hunting season but 16 years later that figure had doubled, reaching 14,910. Based on the number of licences issued, agouti hunting continues to be the most popular form of game hunting, with the number of agouti game licences sold varying between 34.6 % and 45.0 % of the total number of licences. Table 7.11 shows the number of game licences sold by type of game animal species. Given that hunters are required to fill and return the mandatory data form which provides information on harvest statistics, the Wildlife Section is able to estimate the total number of game animals caught and their total weight, and thus estimate the total value of the annual legal harvest. For the 2002/2003 hunting season, sales of state game licences totaled TT$276,500, while the total value of the harvested wild meat was estimated at TT$12.3 million (Table 7.12). However, it should be remembered that this figure only represents a portion of the total value of the industry, given that there is no estimation of other direct and indirect costs associated with hunting and the consumption of wild meat. In addition, there are no estimates of the size and value of illegal hunting which is believed to be extensive.
