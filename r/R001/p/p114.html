5.2 Hydrocarbon Mining 5.2.1 Description The exploitation of marine-based oil and natural gas has increased over the years as indicated in Chapter Four. However, both exploration and mining in the marine environment are taking place in deeper waters, as well as expanding into areas not previously exploited. The east and north coasts of Trinidad are now active mining areas while the north coast of Tobago is currently being considered for future exploration activity. Blocks 21, 22 and 23 (a), (b) and (c) off the north and north-east coasts of Tobago are some of the deepest waters in the nation’s marine environment in which mining operators have turned their attention (Figure 5.3). Water depths range from about 250 m on the southernmost section of Block 21 to over 1900 m in Block 23 (b). This is in comparison to the 50 to 60 m depth in Block 2(c) or the older fields such as Samaan, Poui and Teak on Trinidad’s east coast11. Marine crude reserves were calculated at 10 trillion cubic feet in 1995 as reported by the IMA which also noted that there were 14 offshore producing oil and gas fields at that time (IMA 1999). The number of lease holders increased in 1998 with the auctioning off of new blocks and additional participation by companies such as Petro Canada, BHP Billiton and Talisman. In addition, new gas finds were declared by companies such as BHP Billiton. Unfortunately, data on current reserves, size of leases and annual production figures by companies are not readily available. Table 5.1 lists the companies that are lease holders. Note that Petro Canada was included based on information from a public consultation held in Tobago in 2006 that revealed that Petro Canada was an active lease holder in 1999. There was a general upward trend in natural gas production over the years 1999 to 2004. Annual production for the October 2003 to June 2004 and October 2004 to May 2005 periods recorded 21,714 and 21,819 mcm, respectively.
