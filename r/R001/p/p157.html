Table 7.4: Summary of the State of Wetlands, Coral Reefs and Seagrass Habitat in Trinidad and Tobago (Based on IMA 2006) Resource Location and Extent Ecological Functions Status Wetlands Over 25,000 ha of wetlands; the most significant include:

In Trinidad:  The Caroni Swamp on the west coast, a tidal mangrove swamp of 8,398 ha of marine, estuarine and river alluvial deposits. It contains about 60 % of the mangroves in Trinidad;  The Nariva Swamp on the east coast, which consists of about 6,234 ha and is the largest and most diverse wetland ecosystem in the country;  The South Oropouche (Godineau) Swamp on the west coast which occupies some 3,171 ha;  The North Oropouche (Fishing Pond) Swamp on the east coast which covers an area of 1,220 ha; and  Los Blanquizales Wetland on the south coast which consists of 1,085 ha.

In Tobago:  The Bon Accord Lagoon on the south coast consists of a system of 88.1 ha;  Buccoo Bay freshwater marsh/mangrove swamp which is about 15 ha; and  Kilgwyn Mangrove Swamp, which consists of an area of about 12 ha. Wetlands are important interfaces between land and water resources, and function with coral reefs and seagrass beds in a delicate equilibrium. They serve a protective function for the coastline and reefs by protecting against storm surge, flooding, erosion and tsunamis and by filtering sediments, nutrients and other pollutants before they reach the sea, thus protecting the reefs against siltation and the associated degradation. They provide habitat for avifauna and endangered wildlife (e.g. Scarlet Ibis in the Caroni Swamp and Manatee in the Nariva Swamp) and hence support the ecotourism industry which generates employment and income. Wetlands are also important economically as the nutrients and nurseries which they offer many edible fish species such as finfish, oysters, crabs, lobsters, conch, and shrimps.

In addition, wetlands are important for recreational and educational purposes. About 50 % of the wetlands in Trinidad and Tobago have been lost due mainly to anthropogenic activities, with the resources of the surviving wetlands degraded, through misuse and over exploitation, and conspicuous conflict of interest among the wide variety of resource users.

In 1979, approximately 500 ha of mangrove forest from Couva to an area north of Claxton Bay had been reclaimed for the construction of the Point Lisas Industrial Park and Deep-water Port, while the Mucurapo Swamp at Invader’s Bay was reclaimed for residential and commercial built development.

Large acreages have been converted to agricultural use in the Nariva, South Oropouche and Fishing Pond Swamps.

About 3,600 ha of the Nariva Swamp require restoration due to large scale intensive rice production which has since been stopped.

The wetlands in the Lowlands, Bon Accord, and Roxborough areas, including the Bon Accord Lagoon/Buccoo wetlands and the Kilgwyn Swamp, were reclaimed for coconut and cocoa cultivation during the pre-independence era.
