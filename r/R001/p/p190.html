8.3.3 Response WASA is currently making efforts to improve and upgrade water infrastructure to increase the water supply to the population of Trinidad and Tobago. To achieve its objectives, WASA installed 50 km of pipeline in 2002 and increased this figure to 100 km annually over the last two years. During 2003 to 2005, the Authority has also rehabilitated and constructed production wells and booster stations. One hundred and fifty thousand customers are reported to have benefited from a recently improved water supply. The ultimate goal of WASA is to increase public access to pipe-borne water from 92 % to 95 % and increase the number of customers receiving a 24 hour supply throughout the week from 26 % to 36 % over the next three years (Box 8.2).

Box 8.2: WASA’s Plans for Improving Water Supply (Source: Vision 2020 Planning Committee 2003a)

About 92 % of the population has access to drinking water, although this is at varying levels of service. One of the principal aims of WASA is the upgrading of the water sector so that water services are delivered to the entire population 24 hours a day, seven days a week (24/7). This is part of the National Development Vision to attain developed country status by 2020. At present, 26 % of the population gets a 24/7 water supply while water is supplied to some communities on a weekly schedule. However, there are many communities without a regular water supply. Consequently, over the next two years WASA plans increase access to water from 92 % to 95 % and as such initiatives are being implemented currently to provide a water supply to communities with limited or no access to pipe-borne water and to socially depressed areas.

As part of its development plans, WASA also proposes to increase the number of consumers receiving 24/7 supply of potable water from 26 % to 36 % over the next two years. This is to be achieved through the upgrading and rehabilitation of water infrastructure including the replacement of existing pipelines. At present, new pipelines are being installed at a rate of 100 km per annum with a targeted 150,000 customers receiving an improved water supply between 2003 and 2005.

Apart from the replacement of old pipelines, WASA has undertaken a number of initiatives to reduce unaccounted water. The implementation of the district metered areas (DMA) is the primary method used to address this problem, with 40 DMA established by 2003 (Joseph and Houlder 2003). Metering of individual households is also being considered so that domestic customers will pay for water actually used. In addition, a public relations campaign is usually conducted each year in the dry season focusing on water conservation in that critical period. However, there is a need to extend this campaign throughout the year to include other aspects of water conservation. One response to shortage and intermittent supply of water, on the part of householders and businesses, is the installation and use of high-volume water tanks for storage. Although the use of water tanks is pervasive, there is limited data which provide a true picture of their importance and use. 8.4 Road Travel and Transportation 8.4.1 Introduction/ Overview Transportation is an important element of the economic and social development of Trinidad and Tobago economy. With a significant proportion of the population having to travel outside of their communities to key urban centres to access jobs, schools and government services, Trinidad and Tobago has become a very mobile society. Most travel in Trinidad and Tobago involves private cars and taxis (cars, small and large maxi-taxis). However, traffic congestion is a worsening problem throughout Trinidad and in downtown Scarborough, particularly during peak periods, as the road network is inadequate to handle the volume of vehicles, and no mass transport system exists as an alternative. Movement between Trinidad and Tobago is achieved via air and sea travel and in recent times has improved tremendously with the introduction of new ferries on the Tobago-Trinidad route.
