The main issues identified in respect of the marine environment are hydrocarbon mining, marine transportation, fisheries resources exploitation, coastal erosion and coastal development. Exploitation of the nation’s coastal and marine resources has been long and continuous and has placed increasing pressure on the resource. These include construction and operation of both coastal and marine facilities for winning oil and gas as well as for high income residential and commercial uses; dredging and reclamation works in the near shore and dumping of dredged spoils off shore; over fishing and destructive fishing practices; and discharge of oil, ballast water and waste from marine vessels. Changes in the condition of the resource as a consequence of human actions include an increase in marine and land-based pollution and modification of the shape of the coastline, particularly on Trinidad’s west coast and Tobago’s north-west coast. Other changes include destruction of fish habitats in the Gulf of Paria and elsewhere; resource depletion including declining fish populations and loss of beaches particularly along the south and west coasts of Trinidad; destruction of seagrass beds and coral reefs; and localised changes in inshore water quality and currents. Responses to human activity or resource changes include enactment of local legislation, accession to international conventions and protocols and institutional capacity building to monitor and regulate use of the resource. Local legislation such as the Shipping (Marine Pollution) Bill, No. 2, 2004 (this bill lapsed in September, 2005), the Marine Areas (Preservation and Enhancement) Act Chapter 37:02, (Act No. 1 of 1970, amended in 1996) and the Territorial Sea Act, Chapter 1:51 (Act No. 38 of 1969, amended in 1986) were enacted to extend control over the local marine environment and regulate and control its use. The Water Pollution Rules, once implemented, are expected to control effluent discharge into the environment. The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, the Convention for the Protection and Development of the Marine Environment of the Wider Caribbean Region, the Protocol Concerning Co- operation and Development in Combating Oil Spills in the Wider Caribbean Region, the Protocol Concerning Pollution from Land-based Sources and Activities and the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships are the marine-based international conventions and protocols to which the nation has acceded. Institutional capacity building includes preparation of a Draft Port Plan intended to modernise the port and port activities, along with on-going programmes of support such as subsidies to the fishing industry; construction and maintenance of fisheries infrastructure in several locations by the Municipal Corporations; and construction of coastal defence structures by the Ministry of Works and Transport, private developers and property owners. Inland Waters The resource, inland waters, consists of drainages and impoundment systems, both natural and artificial. Natural systems include rivers, wetlands and other naturally occurring standing water bodies and artesian well systems, while artificial systems comprise man-made ponds and lakes, reservoirs, and irrigation and drainage canals. The islands’ topography, principally the Main Ridge in Tobago and the Northern, Southern and Central Ranges in Trinidad, determines the location and characteristics of the drainage and water impoundment systems. The highlands provide the source of the river systems while the lowlands or basins offer areas of water impoundment. Many of the nation’s natural wetlands are located along the coasts of both islands, and its river systems run from the highlands to the Gulf of Paria, Caribbean Sea or Atlantic Ocean. While most of the natural water systems have been identified and catalogued, the characteristics and locations of many of the reservoirs, weirs and other manmade water impoundment structures remain largely unknown to the public. Surface water sources provide 75 % of the nation’s water supply while the remainder comes from ground water sources.
