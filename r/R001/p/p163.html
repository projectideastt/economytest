In Trinidad, the UNCBD Programme covers three zones, engaging 19 groups each in the North and the South, and 14 in the East. In Tobago the Programme, which falls under the control of the THA, currently works with three communities. 7.3 Species Protection 7.3.1 Description Species protection considers the vulnerability of local flora and fauna to the conditions in Trinidad and Tobago. An important aspect of this is the status of species as it relates to their existence in their natural habitat - whether or not they exist in adequate numbers, are under threat, or rare. Species are classified as endangered, vulnerable or rare. Endangered species are those whose numbers are so low that they are close to extinction, whilst vulnerable species can become endangered in the near future if steps are not taken to correct causal factors leading to reduction in their population levels. Rare species, on the other hand, are those with historically low populations that are at risk. 7.3.2 Pressure The threats and pressures reported in the previous section on Habitat Conservation and Protection are also relevant under this section. These include forest degradation and loss, forest fires, pollution, and declining water quality. Added to these pressures is the over-exploitation of certain species, including timber species, tree ferns, orchids, freshwater fish species and prawns, marine species particularly demersal fish species and sea turtles, cage bird species, and game animals including deer, lappe, quenk, ducks and waterfowl (EMA 1998a). Some of this over-exploitation is culturally based, for example, there is a very high demand for ‘wild meat’ which is consumed locally as delicacy and now also sold commercially. The ‘fear’ of snakes means that they are immediately killed whether they are venomous or not. Some harvesting methods can be destructive, for example the illegal use of trap guns; the setting of fires when hunting game species; the cutting and destruction of mangrove to remove oysters; and the use of bleach in the harvesting of lobsters. Over-exploitation has led to significant reductions in some species population levels, while their populations in some areas have disappeared. For example, the manatee was once found in Caroni Swamp but is now believed to be absent from this area because of over-exploitation.
