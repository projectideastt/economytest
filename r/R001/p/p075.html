Therefore, the public awareness activities and a good communication strategy will continue to be central elements for achieving successful termination of all ODS imports. 3.6 Other Pollutants: Sahara Dust 3.6.1 Background Each year, massive dust storms from in the Sahara and the Sahel deserts occur in Northern Africa creating a reservoir of dust particles over Northern Africa. Strong winds, combined with the rising warm air, lift dust as much as 3,048 to 4,572 m (10,000 to 15,000 feet) into the Saharan Air Layer, forming dense clouds which are transported across the Atlantic (between 10° and 20° North latitude) to the Caribbean, South-eastern USA, and Central and South America. It is estimated that it takes dust particles 5 to 7 days to reach the Caribbean (Mohammed and Agard 2005). The movement of these dust clouds usually coincides with the summer months in the Northern Hemisphere, between February and October. Evidence suggests that movement of dust across Northern Africa has been increasing in recent years because of increased periods of drought and because of desertification. These particles of dust travel across the Atlantic altering air quality and the climate processes, changing soil chemistry and depositing loads of minerals in water bodies. These also affect plant and animal life and create health problems for susceptible humans. Research has shown that these dust particles transport micro-organisms, fungi and chemical contaminants. Sahara dust in the atmosphere is known to cause respiratory problems for asthmatics. 3.6.2 Pressure Saharan dust events are unavoidable. In its 2004 special report on the movement of Sahara dust, the United State's National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) reported that in July 2000, almost eight mmt of Sahara dust reached Puerto Rico and this measurement equaled one-fifth of the total year’s dust deposit. The trans-Atlantic transport of Sahara dust is not a recent phenomenon. However, the amount of material reaching the Caribbean has increased in recent times as well as the composition of the dust is believed to have changed because of human activity. Current data suggest that Africa is experiencing an intensive phase of drought and the concentration of dust reaching the Caribbean region is estimated to be three to four times higher than that experienced in the 1960s (World Health Organisation 2003). 3.6.3 Current State and Trends The presence of Sahara dust in the atmosphere around Trinidad and Tobago has been observed over the years and documented in the first air quality study, carried out over a six week period in 1999 to monitor suspended particulate matter concentrations along the East-West Corridor (Rajkumar and Siung-Chang 2000). The results showed that the daily maximum PM10 concentrations approached the USEPA 24-hour ambient standard of 150 µgm-3 only on days when Sahara dust was present in the atmosphere. These results suggest that Saharan dust events can make up a significant portion of the PM10 concentration in the air and can be an important aspect of ambient air quality.
