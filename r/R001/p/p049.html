The IPCC-endorsed projections equate a likely increase of between 1.0 to 3.5 ºC by 2100, a sea level rise of between 15 and 95 cm and a rainfall deficit of about 15 %. 3.2.4 Current State and Trends In Trinidad, the 30-year average annual rainfall for the period 1975 to 2004 is 1,870 mm. However, depending on location and topography, there can be wide variation among different areas on the island. In the eastern section of the Northern Range rainfall can reach as high as 3,800 mm, and for the same period be as low as 1,200 mm both on the off-shore islands of the northwest peninsula and in the southwest peninsula of Trinidad. The pattern for Tobago is comparable with that of Trinidad in quantity, seasonality and spatial distribution where rainfall ranges between 3,800 mm in the northeast and 1,400 mm in the southwest. For Trinidad, the 30-year mean annual average air temperature varies between 32.6 ºC (in the day) and 20.2 ºC (at nights). Average relative humidity is approximately 80 %, but ranges between 50 % in the dry season and 100 % in the rainy season. During the dry season, there is wide diurnal fluctuation in relative humidity. The Northeast Trades dominate the wind regime, showing great steadiness throughout the year. The average wind speed ranges between 20 to 28 km per hour, with the lowest speeds occurring in the rainy season1. There is considerable local variation in wind velocity, related to a range of factors including topography, the diurnal cycle and thunderstorm activity. Rainfall Figure 3.5 illustrates the 30-year (1975-2004) rainfall distribution pattern for Trinidad and Tobago. Rainfall varies greatly according to the season, with some 67 to 92 % of the total rainfall occurring during the rainy season. The total rainfall varies between 118.9 mm and 854.4 mm during the dry season, and 1,148.2 mm and 1,999.0 mm during the wet season. Figure 3.6 shows that there were generally drier than usual conditions in (a) Trinidad and (b) Tobago during some of the recorded El Niño years (Bourne 2003).
