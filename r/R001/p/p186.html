8.3.1 Pressure In Trinidad and Tobago water is consumed by mainly industrial and domestic users with small volumes consumed for agricultural production. Therefore, unless there is a policy which focuses on improving agricultural production through the use of irrigation, the discussion on water demand should zero in on domestic, industrial, and unaccounted for water. Figures for long-term trends for water demand in Trinidad and Tobago are shown in Table 8.5. These figures reveal that the demand for domestic water will continue to dominate, almost doubling by 2025. The loss of water through illegal connections, faulty pipelines and treatment process (unaccounted for water) is estimated at 41.8 % and 29.2 %, with less because of increased demand. Water demand by industrial users was estimated at 21.6 % in 2005 and expected to increase to 26 % by 2025. The main non-consuming demand for Trinidad and Tobago relates to demand for water. Rivers must maintain a minimum of 20 % of the natural river flow in order to maintain healthy ecosystems. Water wastage is another critical issue affecting water demand and use, which places responsibility on the customer for reducing water use. While many households receive a limited supply of water or none at all, there are others with faulty taps, pipelines, and tanks that go unchecked for considerable periods of time. Water conservation is another important area which needs to be effectively addressed, particularly with respect to the recycling of water (e.g. use of treated wastewater for the hotel sector and other industries); purchase of water saving devices (low-volume shower heads and toilets); and reduction of night use of water through the flushing of toilets and other activities. In their presentation on Leak Reduction in Point Lisas Industrial Estate, Joseph and Houlder (2003) noted that the UK uses 1.77 litres/property/hour while WASA uses 6.0 litres/ property/hour. 8.3.2 Current State and Trends Domestic users will continue to be the largest consumers of water in Trinidad and Tobago for some time to come. World Bank Statistics estimate annual freshwater withdrawals to be 0.3 billion m3. Domestic usage averaged 68 % during the period 1987 to 2002, while industrial activities consumed 26 % of the water withdrawn (Table 8.6). The actual abstractions of surface and groundwater for Trinidad and Tobago in 2000 were 278 million m3 per year and 81.1 million m3 per year respectively (Table 8.7). Unaccounted water is another area which requires further attention, particularly at the district level. Table 8.8 shows the proportion of unaccounted water for several districts in Trinidad, which ranged between 17.7 % and 90.5 % during 2002 to 2003. At present, there is reportedly an annual 14 % deficit in the supply of water and this figure is projected to increase by 27 % by 2015. These deficits persist despite an apparent abundance of water in Trinidad and Tobago and recent investments to improve water supply including the installation of a desalination plant at Point Lisas. This deficit in water supplied to consumers is due to low surface water flows during the dry season and high turbidity of surface water which often lead to increased shutdown at water collection facilities. Recent hydro-geological studies in Tobago and Trinidad have shown additional potential groundwater capacity which can yield supplies through the application of technologies for abstracting deep groundwater in bedrock aquifers. Pollution is another issue affecting water usage. Many water sources are now polluted resulting in increases to the unit cost of producing potable water.
