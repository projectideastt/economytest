Aggregate won through quarrying includes sands and gravels, limestone, porcellanite, clays, tar and oil sands and andesite. In 2004 there were 56 quarries (licenced and unlicenced) operating throughout the country. Minerals used principally in the construction industry are exploited from roughly 5,435 ha spread across the Northern and Central Ranges and the northern and southern basins, with the majority of sand and gravel deposits identified in the Arima to Valencia area of the Northern Basin in Trinidad’s East-West Corridor (MEEI 2005). Impacts of mining are felt in the local communities generally close to the primary source of operations and are often due to land clearing, excavation, wind-driven pollution, ruptured oil lines, abandoned oil mining or transport equipment and transport of material. These have given rise to decreased land capability, reduction in aesthetic/amenity value, modification of landform, land contamination, and water and air pollution. 4.3.2 Pressure Quarry Material The phenomenal expansion of construction especially in the areas of housing, industry, and roads over the last three to four years has led to increased activity in widespread winning of aggregate, such that local production of aggregate more than doubled between 2001 and 2004. It moved from a reported 1.891 mcm to 4.206 mcm over the period. However, mining has modified the landform in large areas of Wallerfield, Valencia and Longdenville by land clearance and open pit mining. Additionally, washing of aggregate has led to silting of several of the rivers close to the location of these quarries, many of which are in the Northern Range while crushing of the aggregate has led to air pollution in Wallerfield and elsewhere. Illegal quarrying is a major source of difficulties particularly in the Wallerfield and Valencia areas where operations have been reported to be executed with intimidation and acts of violence on the residents.
