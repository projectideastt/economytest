Of Trinidad and Tobago’s 5,128,000 hectares of land, agriculture including forestry accounts for 333,000 hectares while land for mining exploitation and reserve accounts for approximately 5,435 hectares. Urbanisation consumes more than 127,000 hectares. The main issues identified in respect of the land are mining for the supply of oil, asphalt and aggregates; agriculture including forestry for food, shelter and industrial materials; urbanisation or human settlement including residential, commercial and industrial spaces; and pollution from the disposal of solid and liquid waste as by-products of human effort and physical development. The country has deposits of asphalt, oil and natural gas, which constitute its hydrocarbon sector as well as mineral deposits, mostly aggregates and clays. These have been exploited over the years, the former mostly for export and the latter principally for usage in the local construction industry. The demand for aggregate has outstripped the local industry's capacity to supply. This demand is fuelled by increasing urbanisation, principally an expansive housing construction programme, construction of office buildings, industrial estates and road infrastructure. Small landholder agricultural production is the main agricultural activity practised since plantation agriculture, such as sugar cane and coconuts, has been in decline over the decades. Nonetheless, agriculture, including forestry, is the single largest user of the resource. Pressure brought to bear on the land includes oil spills from ruptured, leaking and abandoned oil lines, pumps and processing facilities; squatting, slash and burn land clearance methods, bush fires and increasing use of agro-chemicals; change in land use from agriculture to housing, industry and roads; and indiscriminate disposal of solid, liquid and hazardous waste. Changes in the resource as a consequence of human action include de-vegetated hillsides and modification of the land form, which in turn will result in greater frequency of flooding in the floodplains; siltation of streams and rivers; and pollution of land and waterways. Responses to changes in resources and/or human actions include legislation, and programmes and training to modify behaviour. Existing laws have been amended to increase penalties for illegal or unauthorised occupation or use of State lands for mining, agricultural or residential squatting. Additional legislation is pending regarding changes in rules and regulations governing mining operations, especially relevant to the restoration of played-out or abandoned sites. A programme of regularisation of squatters on State lands is being pursued by the Land Settlement Agency to upgrade the physical environment and relocate those that are occupying environmentally sensitive areas. Training programmes for vegetable farmers in the proper use of agricultural chemicals are organised by the Ministry of Agriculture, Land and Marine Resources. Finally, the major oil companies all implement health, safety and environmental programmes to protect employees and the natural environment. Coasts and Oceans Trinidad and Tobago is an archipelagic state and has claimed a marine area of 75,000 square kilometres and a 370.4 kilometre (200 nautical mile) Exclusive Economic Zone. The country's marine environment is influenced by its proximity to South America since it sits on the shoulder of that continent and shares its continental shelf. The country is also situated at the mouth of the Orinoco River. Trinidad and Tobago’s 704 kilometres of coast support wetlands comprising some 25,000 hectares of swamps and marshes, along with ports, piers and other coastal protection structures, while its marine areas contain rather extensive areas of seagrass beds, algal growth, coral reefs and oil and gas deposits. The country's waters are nutrient rich, support a large fish population and are sufficiently shallow to accommodate easy exploration and mining of hydrocarbons.
