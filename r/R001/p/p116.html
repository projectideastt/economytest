5.2.2 Pressure The marine environment is under pressure from the exploration and utilisation of its resources with pollution, resource depletion and physical destruction being major consequences of this exploitation. Pollution from mining of oil and gas and refining land-based facilities (including petrochemicals) adds to the stress placed on the environment by destroying its resources or reducing the efficacy of the services which the resources provide. There are anecdotal reports of frequent oil spills on many parts of Trinidad’s coastlines, particularly in the southern half of the Gulf of Paria where offshore hydrocarbon winning activity is more than 75 years old. While these spills may be small (and many unreported) the net effect may include significant environmental loss. Public consultation notes from several EIA reports completed for oil and gas developments reflect several comments made by fishermen about the pollution caused by the by-products of oil and gas exploitation (i.e., cuttings, muds and produced water). The cuttings and the muds, especially oil-based muds, are often toxic and are responsible for the destruction of living organisms. Data were not readily available on the quantum of cuttings or muds used in the exploration and subsequent winning of oil and gas, nor the produced water generated during operations. Two land-based spills were identified in the Ortoire River and Erin River in 1997 and 1998 (IMA 1999) respectively. Additionally, the Point a Pierre River has been heavily polluted by surrounding industrialisation. The 1999 IMA Report on State of the Marine Environment indicated that varying quantities of hydrocarbon and heavy metal sediments are found in the waters surrounding the islands. Concentrations higher than 200 to 500 nanograms of pollutant per gram of water were found in surface water runoff proximate to natural oil seepage, drilling and refining operations in the Gulf of Paria. The west coast of Trinidad with its long history of marine exploitation, petrochemical plants, agricultural runoff, and urbanisation is the most polluted of the nation’s coasts. The east coast of Trinidad is less polluted but exhibits “… evidence of pollution by petroleum hydrocarbons where oil exploration and production is taking place” (IMA 1999). In the case of Tobago, there is evidence of petroleum hydrocarbon contamination in the marine environment and this is assumed to originate from sources such as spills or oil seepage from Trinidad’s southeast coast, as well as from marine traffic12. 5.2.3 Condition and Trends Exploration and development of the nation’s marine fields are expected to expand into deeper waters on Tobago’s north coast and Trinidad’s east coast as the search for oil and gas intensifies.
