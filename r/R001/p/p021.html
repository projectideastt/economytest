The Report proposes a set of indicators for use with the PSR Model and separates them into actions which can be described as pressure on the resource, state of the resource or response to the pressure or state of the resource in each of the thematic areas described in the report. The environmental indicators are intended to be used to collect relevant information on environmental health and quality, provide greater insights into their condition and trends. The Report points out the different sources of data and notes that there are major gaps in data availability and reporting. It proposes that agreement be reached among stakeholder agencies on both the number and type of indicators to be used across thematic areas now and in the future, and appropriate methodologies to be developed in collection and reporting. Each of the six thematic areas is elaborated on in a separate chapter on the basis of the PSR Model. The overview generally describes the nature of the resource, the role it plays in the global or local environmental system and its utilisation in national development. The issues are explored in terms of determining some of the explicit human actions impacting on the resource (the pressure) which are aiding in the change of the resource. The current condition of the resource (the state) is also assessed, along with the direction of change in human action or in the resource (the trend), and the actions taken or being pursued through private or public intervention to mitigate or ameliorate the change in the resource, or to prevent or decrease continued human action (the response). FINDINGS Atmosphere The atmosphere is described as a blanket of gas surrounding the earth. While it consists of several layers, the two layers closest to the earth’s surface (the troposphere and the stratosphere) are crucial in the maintenance of environmental conditions necessary for continued life on earth. However, human activities (which give rise to vehicular emissions, bush fires and other pollutants) are compromising the ability of these two layers of the atmosphere to function effectively. The main issues identified in respect of the atmosphere are climate variability and change; enhanced green house effects and global warming; air quality (vehicle emissions and particulate pollutants); stratospheric ozone depletion; and Sahara dust. The pressures exerted on the atmosphere were identified as increased concentrations of ozone depleting material; increased atmospheric concentration of anthropogenic greenhouse gases (GHGs) such as carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide; and increased concentration of tropospheric ozone. The changing state or condition of the resource as a consequence of human actions is seen in increased flooding, sea level rise, increase in extreme rain events, increased coastal erosion, and global warming (increased air and ocean temperatures) among others. General reactions or responses to the changing conditions of the atmosphere include both national legislation and ratification of international protocols. At the local level, draft legislation designed to effect the reduction in air pollutants discharged into the atmosphere from stationary sources such as industrial enterprises, have been developed. At the international level, Trinidad and Tobago will formulate cost- effective national programmes designed to improve the quality of local emissions and reduce the emission of greenhouse gases, consistent with the objectives of the Kyoto Protocol, which this country has ratified. In addition, this country will formulate and implement measures to facilitate adequate adaptation to climate change.
