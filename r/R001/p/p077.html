Chapter 4: Land

4.1 Overview Land, described by the UNCCD as the “… terrestrial bio-productive system that comprises soil, vegetation, other biota, and the ecological and hydrological processes that operate within the system” (United Nations, 2007), provides a range of services and products which support human existence. In Trinidad and Tobago, land resources are utilised for agricultural and forestry production, regulation of the atmospheric and water cycles, mineral supply and mining, human settlement and waste disposal. Indeed, land is the foundation of human civilisations and an object of acquisition and conflict over the millennia. Human manipulation of the land to provide food, clothing, shelter and transportation has led to a growing number of challenges to its capacity to continue to provide services and products in ever increasing quantity and variety. The actual changing of aspects of the land and its processes are threatening the very survival of humankind and has led over time to a more critical look at the way land resources are utilised. As a result, there is increasingly universal recognition of the need for a different approach to land utilisation in order to reduce the negative impacts or mitigate those that are already threatening the Earth. Trinidad and Tobago has accepted the need to act in concert with the countries of the Caribbean Region and the rest of the world in finding and implementing solutions to the problems which currently challenge the land and its natural processes. To this end, the nation has assented to a number of international treaties and protocols that have implications to land management such as the UNCCD, the UNCBD (United Nations, 1992), the Convention on Wetlands of International Importance (Ramsar Convention) and the Protocol for Specially Protected Areas and Wildlife. The archipelagic Republic of Trinidad and Tobago consists of 5,128 km2 (5,128,000 ha) with Trinidad accounting for 4,828 km2 (4,828,000 ha) and Tobago for 300 km2 (300,000 ha) or 5.9% of the land mass.

Trinidad extends 80 km from north to south and 59 km from east to west and contains five physiographic regions, namely: the Northern, Central and Southern Ranges and the intervening lowlands called the Northern and Southern Basins (Figure 4.1).
