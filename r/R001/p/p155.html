Tobago is still classified as forest land, although large portions have been under severe pressure for alternative uses. The accuracy of this 2003 figure and the quality of the country’s forest resources in today’s context are questionable. The estimations are based on the last forest inventory which was conducted over 25 years ago using 1969 aerial photographs. Nonetheless, over the last 20 years, there has been considerable forest depletion in some of the country’s reserves including the Victoria-Mayaro Reserve, Morne L’Enfer Reserve, Erin Reserve, Long Stretch Reserve, and Valencia Reserve. Given the significant level of deforestation none can argue against the need for a new forest inventory which would give a more accurate picture of the status of these invaluable resources. Freshwater Habitat In Trinidad, the main river systems are the Caroni River in the north, the Caparo River in central region, the Ortoire River in the southeast, and the Oropouche River in the southwest. In Tobago, the Courland River is the main river system, traversing almost two thirds of the island. These freshwater systems directly support human activities as well as plant and animal life. Within the last 10 years, a number of studies on the freshwater quality of individual systems have been conducted, all of which point to the impact human activities are having on the main river systems, and thus give an indirect measure of ecological health. However, the status of these systems as freshwater habitats and the life they support have not been recently assessed from an ecological point of view. Coastal and Marine Habitat Table 7.3 provides some basic information about coastal and marine biodiversity in Trinidad and Tobago. Coral reefs, wetlands, and seagrass beds are the most important coastal ecosystems in Trinidad and Tobago. They provide breeding grounds, hatcheries, nurseries, and protection for hundreds of species of finfish and shellfish of ecological and commercial value (IMA 2006), and protect the coastline from erosion, flooding, storm surges and tsunamis. These ecosystems are also habitat to some of the country’s endangered species. For example, the beaches on the northeast Trinidad coast at Matura, Grande Riviere and Fishing Pond; and on the southwest coast of Tobago at Bon Accord and Courland Bay provide nesting habitat for the leatherback and other marine turtle species. Little Tobago and Saut d’Eau Island provide habitat for nesting migratory and other marine bird species, while the Nariva Swamp is habitat to the endangered manatee.
