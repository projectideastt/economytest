Of the 7,125 ha of land under illegal occupation or agricultural squatting recorded countrywide, Trinidad accounted for roughly 6,840 ha or 96% of the land, while Tobago accounted for about 285 ha or 4%. Squatting on agricultural lands was reported to be one of the main causes of deforestation in Trinidad (Kairi Consultants Limited 1999). Agricultural squatting for the most part leads to poor land management practices due to insecurity of tenure, and often results in deterioration of soil and increasing soil infertility. Moreover, as many squatters occupy agricultural lands principally for residences, settlements develop which contain little or no infrastructure such as sewage disposal, garbage disposal, and running water. A variety of long term and short term crops are cultivated on these agricultural squatters’ plots ranging in size from <1 to >100 ha on both State and private lands. However, this practice is not restricted to subsistence farmers, since commercial producers are also known to illegally occupy land. Poor Agricultural Practices Slash and burn land clearance methods and/or shifting cultivation are practised in both Trinidad and Tobago. In slash and burn, fire is used to dispose of vegetative materials and over time, this practice produces fire climax vegetation. Although there are no data on the number and size of fire climax areas, evidence is to be found in the Moriah to Plymouth areas in the Courland watershed in Tobago and several watersheds (Diego Martin, Cascade and Caura) in the Northern Range, Trinidad. Additionally, the system of shifting cultivation, once sustainable, currently does not allow adequate elapse time for the cultivated area to fully recover, and with shorter periods of fallow, degradation of the land increases. This technique is prevalent in Trinidad’s Northern and Central Ranges. Most of the farmers who conduct illegal agriculture and who use techniques of slash and burn or shifting cultivation practice little or no soil conservation, and most farmers cultivating on sloping soils do not practice appropriate soil conservation methods. An example of this is noted in central Trinidad where farmers are known to till and place crops up and down the slope as opposed to along the contour. Additionally, the cultivation of short crops on slopes in the Northern Range and the Courland River Valley generally leaves these soils unprotected for long periods. Failure to employ the correct methods increases soil erosion and sediment load in runoff water. Unfortunately current data are not available on soil loss or rate of soil erosion by soil types since the last report was prepared more than 30 years ago. Some farmers indicated that they utilise fires to burn vegetable matter cleared for cultivation and to release ash into the earth as a fertiliser. However, some of these fires, which are poorly controlled, frequently result in the burning of much larger tracts of land. This practice often leaves the land exposed to the erosive power of rainfall before crops are fully established. A study conducted by the Forestry Division in the Maracas Valley between 1984 and 1989 (Faizool 2002), for example, has shown that land under cultivation is 279 times more prone to erosion than land under natural forest cover. The same report found that land under burnt forest plantation is 19 times more susceptible to land erosion than land under un-burnt forest plantation. Inappropriate Use of Agricultural chemicals Agricultural chemicals are used for pest and weed control and soil fertilisation. However, pesticides are used pervasively in vegetable and tree crop farming while fertilisers are used heavily in the production of sugarcane and vegetables. Data obtained from the Food and Agricultural Organisation (FAO) indicate that fungicides, herbicides and insecticides imported into the country between 1988 and 2003 were valued at TT$52,509,000 (Table 4.16). There has been no significant decrease in use of pesticides over the years as vegetable farmers continue to rely heavily on their use.
