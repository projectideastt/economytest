The population’s attitudes towards waste and waste disposal apparently do not lend to effective waste management. It has been argued that the country’s problems associated with poor solid waste management start with people’s negative perception and hands-off approach to waste. While there has been talk about recycling, the citizenry does not actually place value on waste or proper waste disposal, and there is the general perception that waste is somebody else’s business once it leaves the home or business compound. The existing legal framework does not comprehensively address the issue of inadequate waste management. The problem of environmental pollution caused by solid waste is visibly evident throughout the island. In 1995, SWMCOL estimated that about 85% of solid waste was disposed of at the public landfills. It is likely that this figure has increased given the level of indiscriminate dumping of garbage and debris throughout the country, which obstruct drains and clog rivers, particularly those in the urbanised municipalities along the East-West and Southern Corridors. Litter abounds in many secluded public spaces – beaches, parks, etc. – creating not only land degradation problems but also obnoxious odours and public health hazards. Illegal dumping has become a common practice with over 300 informal reported dump sites throughout Trinidad.

Hazardous Waste The 2003 National Hazardous Waste Inventory undertaken by the Caribbean Environmental Health Institute (CEHI) in 2005/200610 under contract to the EMA, noted that approximately 11,452.5 tonnes of hazardous waste was generated in 2003 by 285 companies, in 27 waste streams as categorised under the Basel Convention on Trans-boundary Movement of Hazardous Waste and their Disposal (CEHI 2006). Seven waste streams generated over 100,000 kg of waste each in 2003 and this is highlighted in Table 4.24. The single largest waste stream (categorised as Y9 by Basel) was generated across all industrial sectors with the largest generator being the oil and gas sector. Companies engaged in the oil and gas sector and the chemical and chemicals product manufacturing sector together generated the largest quantity of hazardous waste in 2003, representing 85.5% of all reported hazardous waste in 2003. Clinical waste from medical care in hospitals, medical centres and clinics) was the second largest quantity followed by waste from organic compounds (Table 4.23).
