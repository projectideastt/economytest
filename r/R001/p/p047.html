According to the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), the mean global surface temperature has increased by about 0.3 to 0.6 ºC since the late 19th century and by about 0.2 to 0.3 ºC over the last 40 years, which is the period with the most reliable data. Recent years have been among the warmest since 1860 - the date from which instrumental recording of sea surface and land-based surface air temperatures began. Warming is evident in both sea surface and land-based surface air temperatures (UNEP/Grid-Arendal 2006a). In fact, 10 of the last 11 years (spanning the period 1995 to 2005), have been among the warmest years since instrumental record keeping of sea surface and land-based surface air temperatures began (IPCC 2007). Rainfall patterns which deviate from the norm can result in drought and flooding. Though regional differences have led to increased rainfall in certain locations and decreased rainfall elsewhere (Figure 3.4), precipitation averaged over the Earth’s land surface increased from the start of the century up to about 1960, but has decreased since about 1980 (UNEP/Grid-Arendal 2006a).

The measurement and collection of climatic data for Trinidad began in the 1940s, while such measurements in Tobago only started in the mid 1960s. This makes it difficult to determine with some degree of accuracy if our local climate changes reflect the global trends. However despite the lack of longer term historical data, we can no longer take our climate for granted nor can we wait decades to play the odds in deciding whether climate change is “real” and the ways we should respond to the issue. The very first notions that human activities might cause climate change on regional scales came from British and French island colonies. The administrations of Tobago, St Vincent, St Helena and Mauritius were persuaded in the 18th and early 19th centuries that deforestation was linked to declines in rainfall. This led to protection of the islands’ forest resources (Sear et al. 2001). Some 200 years later, most people would now concur that human activities are accelerating global climate change.
