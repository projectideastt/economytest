A socio-economic survey of vegetable farmers’ practices in Trinidad carried out by the Centre for Agricultural Bioscience International (CABI) and the Ministry of Agriculture, Land and Marine Resources (MALMR) in 1995, reported excessive and unwarranted pesticide applications (Kairo et. al. 2000). Yet, the reports also showed that farmers are aware of the negative health impacts of pesticide use on humans and animals. The scenario remains the same today, with a wide range of pesticides being used, and the same pesticides being used on several crops to control different pests. Pesticide run-off has had negative effects on freshwater sources and the coastal environment. Some pesticides are known to persist in the soil, reaching toxic levels, which can limit soil productivity and fertility. Paraquat, for example, remains one of the most frequently used herbicides in Trinidad and Tobago, despite its property of binding strongly to clays and its persistence in some soils for up to 13 years (Kairi Consultants Limited 1999). A similar picture is painted for fertiliser use. An average of 6,593 tonnes of fertiliser was consumed in Trinidad and Tobago during the period 1993 to 2002 (Table 4.17). Existing data show that fertiliser consumption declined over the period 1997 and 2001, and this is most probably due to a considerable decline in sugar cane production over the same period. However, fertiliser use in vegetable production remains high. Farmers often do not apply fertilisers on a timely basis in the quantities required and, therefore, over-fertilisation continues to be a problem in vegetable production. Fertiliser use in an agricultural study showed that nitrate ions (very mobile) and ammonium ions (less mobile) in nitrogen or mixed fertilisers can move to a depth of 45 cm in a sandy loam soil within one week after nitrogen fertiliser application. Of the three fertiliser groups (nitrogenous, phosphate and potash) imported over the years, the country’s average monthly consumption declined from 6,800 tonnes in 1998 to 3,258 tonnes in 2002 with the greatest consumption in nitrogenous fertilizers (Table 4.17).
