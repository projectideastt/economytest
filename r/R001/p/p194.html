8.4.3 State and Trends Travel Patterns and Traffic Volume The predominant movement of vehicular traffic is between settlements/communities and urban or town centres. To date, there has been no comprehensive study on travel patterns in Trinidad and Tobago. Instead information on travel patterns is derived from sporadic and specific measurements geared at addressing targeted issues. However anecdotal evidence suggests that private cars play a major role in the journey to work, school, and social activities. The public transportation system consists mainly of taxis and small and large maxi taxis. A public bus service operated by the Public Transport Service Corporation, also plays a significant role, offering services mainly to and from main urban centres and outer districts/communities, for example the East-West Corridor, North-South Corridor, and to and from San Fernando to areas such Mayaro, Point Fortin and Siparia. The technical report on Greater Port of Spain Local Area Plan (Halcrow Group et al. 2000) indicated that in 1996 just over 200,000 people were estimated to be travelling into the city area daily, with about 40,000 during peak hours, with an estimated 50 % of these commuters using public transport. It was estimated that by 2000, approximately 500,000 persons daily were travelling into and out of Port of Spain, which is equivalent to about 100,000 car trips and 20,000 public transport trips. The Port of Spain traffic trend therefore seems to be increasing every year. Congestion The current rate of vehicle ownership and traffic growth has placed great demands on the national transportation infrastructure resulting in gridlock in many of the country’s main roadways and access roads particularly, along the main East-West and North-South arterial roads in Trinidad, leading to loss of productivity and frustration among the travelling public. The once 30 to 45 minute journey from Arima to Port of Spain can now take as much as one and a half to two hours during peak hours, and the 45 minute to one hour trip from San Fernando to Port of Spain can now extend up to two and a half hours. Many commuters are now forced to leave their homes at earlier hours than they have done in the past traversing back streets and roads to avoid the bulk of the traffic. Moreover traffic bottlenecks are no longer a weekday phenomenon — they can now also be experienced on weekends and public holidays.
