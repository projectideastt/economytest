Experience has shown that there are several underlying factors in Trinidad and Tobago which lead to negative environmental change. Among the major ones identified in the MPUE's State of Land Resources of Trinidad and Tobago (MPUE 2005), are: • Population pressures; • Rising affluence; • Poverty; • Approach to development; • Incompatible land and marine use; • Insufficient institutional capacity; • Inadequate legislation; • Insufficient monitoring and enforcement of existing laws; • Inadequate stakeholder collaboration (among State agencies and the private and NGO sectors); and • Negative elements of popular culture. 2.2.1 Population Pressures Urbanisation and rural to urban drift have resulted in population concentrations in the built up areas in the East-West and North-South corridors of Trinidad and in southwest Tobago. Such population pressures are leading to increased densification of traditional urban neighbourhoods (for example, St James, Woodbrook, Maraval, Diego Martin, Tunapuna, Maracas and St Augustine), where there is an increasing trend to replace single family units with apartment buildings and townhouse complexes. Chaguanas is the fastest growing urban centre in the country, with an increasing number of new satellite communities emerging in the region (CSO 2000). This densification of traditional neighbourhoods and the expansion of settlements into new areas have not always occurred with a corresponding expansion and upgrading of infrastructure and amenities. The resulting situation is inadequate drainage systems, sewerage systems, and local road networks in commercial and built up areas. Urban centres such as Port of Spain, San Fernando, Chaguanas and Arima support a large transient population, but nowhere is this population greater than Port of Spain where during weekdays its transient population is at least five times the size of the city’s resident population of about 50,000 (CSO 2000). This high transient population places a burden on existing infrastructure and sanitation services, which leads to environmental degradation. 2.2.2 Rising Affluence For many societies, decoupling the link between affluence and consumption remains the key challenge as increased consumption is often linked to increased waste generation and other environmental problems. For Trinidad and Tobago, economic growth has created increased demand for the country’s resources, rising levels of expectation and increased consumption of goods and services. This is particularly evident in the expansion of the vehicle population on the nation’s roads. Figures indicate that during the period 1995 to 2005, the number of registered vehicles in the country had almost doubled to just over 400,000 cars (CSO 2007). This has implications for carbon dioxide emissions and declining air quality. It is reported that global carbon dioxide emissions from motor vehicles (of which emissions from cars make up 74%), increased during 1990 to 1997 by 26% and at a rate four times greater than the growth of carbon dioxide emissions overall (Myers and Kent 2003).
