Nonetheless, based on the inventory and as reported in the 2001 Initial National Communication under the UNFCCC (EMA 2001b), the total anthropogenic carbon dioxide emissions are estimated at 14,988 Gg and non carbon dioxide emissions at 2,561 Gg. Thus, carbon dioxide is the main GHG being emitted (95.4 % of global warming potential [GWP]), followed by methane (4.5 % of GWP). According to the 2001 Initial National Communication, the energy sector was the main contributor of GHG emissions. The combustion of fossil fuel for the production of energy contributed 66 % of total GHG emissions, with industrial processes accounting for the remaining 34 %. Within the energy sector, energy industries accounted for 44 % of the total emissions, with manufacturing and construction industries contributing 38 %, transportation 15 % and other activities 3 %. The main source of methane emissions came from waste, followed by 18 % from agriculture and 11 % from other sources. It was estimated that 62 % of nitrous oxide emissions came from agriculture and 34 % from waste. The 2001 Initial National Communication also indicated that land use change and forestry (where forests serve as greenhouse sinks) removed anthropogenic GHG emissions by 10.2 %. However, this was reportedly due largely to abandonment of agricultural holdings and estates. The recent increase in deforestation and land clearance may reverse this trend. Estimations some four years later (in 1994) painted a picture of increased net GHG emissions and changes in the sources of these emissions. An EMA report on the Environmental and Human Health Effects of Vehicular Exhaust (EMA unpublished report) showed that carbon dioxide emissions for 1994 remained the highest, approximately 683 % higher than the 1990 estimates (Table 3.3). By all counts, individual contribution of the various gases to total GHG emissions from road transportation has increased significantly and has become the major source of anthropogenic nitrous oxides, nitrogen oxides, non-methane volatile organic compounds (NMVOCS) and carbon monoxide. While in 1990 road transport contributed less than 10 % of carbon dioxide and methane emissions, by 1994 the sector was responsible for more than 30 % of total emissions. Such emissions can be expected to increase further with the increasing number of vehicles on the country’s roads.
