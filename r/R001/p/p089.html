No information was readily available on the number and locations of abandoned oil wells which are leaking and need to be properly decommissioned. 4.3.3 Current State and Trends Quarry Material The State anticipated that beyond 2005 it would be able to provide one year licences to most of the quarry operators. Thus some 60 mining operations, 57 in Trinidad and 3 in Tobago, should be awarded leases on approximately 2,700 ha of land in north, south and central Trinidad and Tobago for the mining of sand and gravel, pitrun gravel and blue limestone. It is expected that few licences will be given out for minerals such as silica sand, oil sand and andesite. The quarry industry was unable to respond to the increasing demands for its products given its current capacity. Thus, new quarry sites are likely to be put on the market to satisfy a demand for aggregate which will increase over the next few years as the Government’s road expansion programme and its housing construction accelerate. There are no reliable data on new quarry sites being opened, size of sites and annual production in respect of mining of aggregate and, given the reported widespread operations of illegal quarrying operations, accurate data are unlikely to be available in the short term. Additionally, there are no data on rehabilitation of quarry lands on which materials have been exhausted. Land currently mined is located in the Northern Range and its Northern Basin from Santa Cruz to Valencia; in the central plains from Carlsen Field to Longdenville; and from Erin to Guapo in the Southern Basin. In Tobago, major mining activity is located in Studley Park. No data are available on the size of the area disturbed in the extraction of aggregate. Hydrocarbons Domestic crude oil production exhibited peaks and troughs over the years but appears to be trending upwards (Table 4.9). With sustained high oil prices and “technology optimism”, land-based production can improve as once-uneconomic wells are returned to production. Indeed, like the Tax Incentive Act of 1976, which was intended to encourage the application of secondary and tertiary recovery methods in land-based production of crude oil, new technologies are expected to be utilised to extract oil from land-based wells that were once considered uneconomic. But limited oil resources and expensive technologies could cause the “Peak Oil” effect, i.e. when the maximum rate of petroleum extraction is reached, after which the rate of production enters terminal decline.
