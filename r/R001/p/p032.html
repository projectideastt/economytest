While the PSR Model makes a positive link between the environment and humans through resource use, what this model does not do is elaborate on the ways in which various aspects of our well- being are enhanced or affected by different environmental services. In the 2004 State of the Environment Report which focused on the Northern Range, the MA conceptual framework was used. Although principally designed for the assessment of the state of ecosystems through an examination of ecosystem services, this conceptual framework introduced for the first time a breakdown of the constituents of human well-being and made the linkages between these, ecosystem services, and the drivers of environmental change (human and natural, both directly and indirectly affecting ecosystem change). Since its introduction, the MA conceptual framework has been found to be extremely useful in environmental reporting, and is gaining credibility for reporting not only from an ecosystem standpoint. A more in-depth explanation of this framework is provided in the 2004 State of the Environment Report and can also be accessed at www.maweb.org. Although the 2005 ASOE focuses on the PSR Model, future reports will combine the information in the PSR Model and MA conceptual framework to more effectively make the links to human well-being. In addition, elements of the United Nations Environment Programme’s (UNEP) Driving Force-Pressure- State-Impact-Response (DPSIR) model will also be incorporated (UNEP/Grid-Arendal 2008). 1.5 Report Preparation A Steering Committee was established to provide technical guidance and input into the preparation of the 2005 ASOE. This Committee was convened at the beginning of the process, and before finalisation of the Report, members were required to review the report for scientific accuracy and rigour. The Committee was comprised of members drawn from several government agencies such as: the Department of Natural Resources and the Environment (DNRE) of the Tobago House of Assembly (THA); the Ministry of Public Utilities and the Environment (MPUE); the Institute of Marine Affairs (IMA); the EMA; the Water and Sewerage Authority (WASA)/Water Resources Agency (WRA); and the Ministry of Trade and Industry (MTI). One non-government organisation (NGO), the Cropper Foundation, was also involved. Data and information used to compile this report were collected from a range of sources, including published and unpublished documents, and through interviews with experts and national stakeholders. Annex 2 provides a breakdown of sources of information from various agencies and ministries. 1.6 Reporting Themes Six thematic areas have been selected in reporting on the Trinidad and Tobago environment: Atmosphere; Land; Coasts and Oceans; Inland Waters; Biodiversity; and Economy, Industry and Resource Use. These themes are defined as follows: Atmosphere The sum total of all gases surrounding the earth, extending several hundred km above the surface in a mechanical mixture of various gases in fluid-like motion (EMA 2001a). The atmosphere is artificially divided into layers with the troposphere (closest to the earth) and the stratosphere (found above the troposphere) being the areas of greatest environmental influence. Land The terrestrial bio-productive system that comprises soil, vegetation, other biota, and the ecological and hydrological processes that operate within the system (MPUE 2006).
