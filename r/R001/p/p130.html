In the case of Tobago, the Main Ridge, an area of highland running for about two-thirds of the length of the island in a south-westerly to north-easterly direction, is the source for the island’s main river systems. Five of these rivers drain to the north, namely: Bloody Bay, Castara, Englishman’s Bay, Parlatuvier and Courland Rivers. Draining south into the Atlantic Ocean are the Richmond, Goldsborough and Hillsborough Rivers. 6.1.2 The Basins The two basins known as the Northern and Southern Basins are located between the three mountain ranges. The Northern Basin extends from the foothills of the Northern Range to those of the Central Range and consists of: the floodplains and alluvial flats of the Caroni and North Oropouche River systems; the Northern Terraces (which lies north of the Caroni and North Oropouche rivers and east of Arouca); and the Las Lomas Peneplain (which occupies the Manzanilla District in the southeast of the region). The Southern Basin consists of the Naparima Peneplain. The landscape of this basin is gently rolling, reaching its highest point in the San Fernando Hills. The peneplain is dissected by numerous small streams which are dry in the dry season and have high runoff rates in the wet season. The main drainage systems are the Poole, Guaracara and South Oropouche Rivers. The flatlands of Tobago occupy one third of the island and are located in the south west. They consist of a flat coral limestone platform that extends seaward to form the offshore coral reefs. 6.1.3 The Wetlands There are several significant wetlands in Trinidad and Tobago (Figure 6.1). The largest and most diverse wetland in Trinidad is the Nariva Swamp which is a freshwater swamp located on the southern section of the east coast. This wetland is a complex of freshwater marsh, freshwater swamp forest, palm forest and an eastern fringe of mangrove forest. Because of its ecological importance, Nariva Swamp was declared a Ramsar site in 1992. The Ramsar area, site No. 577, which covers approximately 11,343 ha, supports a rich fauna of many threatened species, including the globally threatened manatee Trichechus manatus. The site is very important for several species in Trinidad and Tobago, and is critical for the maintenance of the biological diversity in the region. Although it has long been an area of small scale mixed farming activity and artisanal fishing, in the recent past, it was subjected to tremendous pressure from large-scale intensive rice farming which has since been brought under control. The North Manzanilla and the Manzanilla Windbelt are both systems of estuarine mangrove also found on the east coast and together occupy several hectares. In addition, the North Oropouche Swamp occupies a small area at the mouth of the North Oropouche River. On the north coast of Trinidad, the largest wetland (approximately 25 ha) is the freshwater swamp forest found at Maracas Bay. Mangrove swamps are also dominant on Trinidad’s south coast. The largest of these is Los Blanquizales which consists of approximately 840 ha. In general, wetlands along this coast have been only slightly modified by human activity. On the west coast the main swamps are: the Caroni in the north; the Couva River/Carli Bay in the central; the South Oropouche and Roussillac in the south; and the Cedros in the southwest. Caroni Swamp, which is the largest mangrove swamp in Trinidad and Tobago, consists of 8,398 ha (www.ramsar.org/cda/en/ramsar-pubs-annolist-annotated-ramsar-15817/main/ramsar/1-30- 168%5E15817_4000_0) and is an important critical habitat for a number of bird species, including the scarlet ibis (Eudocimus ruber), Trinidad’s national bird.
