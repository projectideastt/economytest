Despite its limited land space, Trinidad and Tobago is endowed with a wide natural resource base compared to other small island developing states (SIDS) of similar size elsewhere in the tropics. Its minerals, soils, waters, flora, fauna, ecosystems and biodiversity are all utilised for the socio-economic development of the country. This combination of natural resources is all captured in diverse landscapes and waterscapes, which add to Trinidad and Tobago’s value as an emerging tourist destination. With a combined oil and natural gas reserve of 9.2 billion barrels of oil equivalent in 2000 (Williams 2003), the energy and petrochemical industries play a dominant role in the development of the national economy.

Without a doubt, the country’s progress and its natural resources are closely inter-related and inter- dependent. With the strength of the economy closely tied to its natural resource base, monitoring and reporting on the health of the environment and the individual natural resources is a key strategy for sustainable development. 2.2 Main Drivers Influencing Environmental Change The arrival of the Europeans to the shores of Trinidad and Tobago in 1498 was the beginning of significant changes in the country’s environment. As a settled colony, its mixture of mountains, valleys, plains and fertile soils made the islands an ideal location for production of export crops. Today, the importance of agricultural production to the economy of Trinidad and Tobago has declined considerably and so the sector is no longer the prime economic driver. Instead the economy is now centred on the exploration of the country’s oil and natural gas resources. Over the years due mainly to increasing and inappropriate human activities, the country’s ecosystems and natural resources have been altered considerably, resulting in a number of negative impacts, some of which are irreversible. The international community has long recognised the relationship between environmental change and socio-economic activities and the need to understand the factors, both direct and indirect, that can lead to such change.
