Therefore, maintaining a sizeable recharge zone is critical. The Northern Gravels, which comprise those aquifers east of Port of Spain to just outside Arima and southward onto the Caroni Plains, are made up of alluvial deposits and gravel fans along the southern foothills of the Northern Range. The rivers in the valleys between Port of Spain and Arima recharge the aquifers as they flow over them on their way to the Caroni River. These aquifers are connected laterally. The Central Sands are located on the southern section of the Northern Basin and extend in irregular bands in a diagonal north-easterly direction from Claxton Bay to Cumuto and dip in a north- westerly direction towards the Gulf of Paria. The Southern Sands are multiple-sand aquifers divided into the Erin Formation and Morne L’Enfer Formation. While most of the aquifers are exploited, it is the Northern Gravels and Northwest Peninsula Gravels that are the top producers. Since Tobago is largely composed of igneous and metamorphic rocks, it is not considered rich in groundwater resources. However, recent hydro-geological studies in 2000 (Maharaj 2005) have identified the presence of substantial water resources in fracture systems of deep bedrock aquifers, which are different from the aquifers to which we often refer. 6.3.2 Pressure The aquifers are under pressure from a range of activities, including agricultural chemical usage, urban development, vegetation removal and forest fires. The application of agricultural chemicals to pest control and crop production has been shown elsewhere to be pervasive. This has generally led to the movement of pollutants into the soils and into both surface and ground water sources. Urban development on the foothills and slopes of the ranges in Trinidad and Tobago has been responsible for the depletion of vegetative cover, significant alteration of the natural drainage system, increased waste disposal loads and attendant pollution, increased soil compaction and reduction of the area available for water percolation. This has led to reduction in water infiltration rates and therefore the recharge of the aquifers is threatened. It also leads to increased surface runoff, especially during periods of intense rainfall, leading to a higher incidence of flooding in low lying areas. The increase in the development of hard surfaces, such as roads, roofs, concrete aprons and parking lots on account of urban development has not been quantified nor even systematically identified. From observation of the number and size of green field sites being used in implementation of the Government’s housing programme, industrial and commercial building construction and office buildings, there has been a phenomenal decrease in the land space available for water percolation and thus aquifer recharge. While more detailed information is available on forest and bush fires it should be noted that other factors contributing to land degradation are dealt with in Chapter Four. Forest and bush fires which also aid in de-vegetating large areas of land are a source of stress on the sub-surface water systems. A forest fire is an unplanned fire, which burns vegetation on lands under forest cover, including grasslands, which were once under forests, or found within forest reserves. The incidence of fires, that is, the number of fires and total area burnt, depends on the severity of the dry season and the level of enforcement by authorities.
