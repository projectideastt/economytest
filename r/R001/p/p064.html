Currently Trinidad and Tobago has no national policy on the limitation or reduction of the carbon dioxide emissions and other GHGs or on climate change. However, given the world’s current heightened awareness and sensitivity to climate change and GHG emissions, many countries and critics to the Kyoto Protocol are calling for an alternative which may be more inclusive of both developed and developing countries. 3.4 Air Quality - Vehicular Emissions and Particulate Pollutants 3.4.1 Background Air pollution has become a major concern to the people of Trinidad and Tobago given the adverse impacts air pollutants may have on human health and other life-forms. The 2000 State of the Environment Report (EMA 2001) defines air pollution as the contamination of the atmosphere by any toxic or radioactive gases and particulate matter as a result of human activity. Therefore air pollutants, which can be produced from people-made or natural sources, are substances whose presence in the atmosphere cause or are likely to cause harm to human, plant and/or animal life; damage human-made materials and structures; bring about changes in weather or climate; or interfere with the enjoyment of life or property. Table 3.4 provides information on the health effects of various air pollutants. In Trinidad and Tobago, air pollution is caused mostly by emissions from motor vehicles and energy based industries. Forest and bush fires, the annual burning of the sugar cane crop during the harvest, and the burning of landfills are also sources of air pollutants. These air borne particles vary in size, with the smaller sized particles remaining suspended in the atmosphere for a considerable period of time. The larger particles may eventually settle out, coating trees, soil and hard surfaces. Air pollutants may also obscure visibility and create unsightly images as they coat buildings and other structures. Low visibility tends to occur during the dry season when there are more frequent bush and forest fires and landfill sites are known to be on fire. Bush fires can be more problematic during El Niño years. 3.4.2 Pressure Motor vehicle exhaust is a complex mixture of gases, the composition of which depends on the quality and type of fuel, design, and engineering of the vehicle, age and population of vehicles, operating conditions of the engine, maintenance programmes, and use of emission control devices (EMA unpublished report). Most passenger type vehicles and some light trucks are equipped with spark- ignition engines, which utilise mainly gasoline, although some may be reconfigured to use compressed natural gas (CNG). Spark-ignition type engines emit air pollutants including hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides and a small amount of particulates. Most heavy trucks, buses and maxi-taxis and some cars in Trinidad and Tobago use diesel fuel engines, which could be up to 100 % more fuel efficient than gasoline type engines. As a result, lower specific emissions of carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons are reported, with greater amounts of nitrogen oxides (particularly nitric oxide and nitrous oxide) emitted due to the higher combustion temperatures and excess oxygen. There can be a significant amount of particulate matter, as evident by black exhaust smoke, which primarily consists of carbon based soot, sulphates and hydrocarbons adsorbed onto the soot particles. With the current trend in the increase in the amount of vehicles in the country, one can expect greater fuel consumption with an accompanied increase in the emission of pollutant gases, as well as associated effects on the environment and human health. These impact projections can be mitigated to some extent if cleaner, more efficient fuels are used and if vehicle exhaust standards are enforced. 3.4.3 Current State and Trends At present the EMA has established an air quality monitoring station at the Point Lisas Industrial Estate to inform and track trends on particular air pollutants.
