Forest Degradation and Loss Natural forests support a significant proportion of the country’s biodiversity. Removal of forested ecosystems reduces and degrades available habitat, feeding areas and other resources which are required to sustain the country’s flora and fauna and, thus, its biodiversity. Given the link between natural vegetative cover and biodiversity, loss of forest cover can be used as an indicator of habitat quality and loss of biodiversity. At present, there are limited data for assessing the loss of individual forest species. Most information generated locally on land use change and forest cover is dated, and thus questionable given the dynamic nature of the country’s development over the last 10 years and the high accessibility of our forests. Nonetheless, every year forest cover is lost to human activity. The EMA’s 2001/2002 State of the Environment Report, utilising international databases37, reported an annual loss of forested area of 0.8 % between 1995 and 2000 (Agard and Gowrie 2003). This figure was again quoted in the 2003 International Tropical Timber Council Report (ITTO) on Forestry in Trinidad and Tobago which also reported that deforestation accounted for the annual loss of about 2,200 ha of natural forests per year, or 0.8 % per year, during the period 1990 to 2000 (ITTO 2003). Though the level of confidence in the estimate was low, Trinidad and Tobago is rated high (at seven) on the Environmental Vulnerability Index scale, making it highly vulnerable to the loss of natural vegetated area. Table 7.1 summarises the state of some of the key forest reserves in Trinidad and Tobago. While in the past agricultural production was primarily responsible for the clearing of natural vegetation, today forest loss is attributed to over-exploitation, industrial development, quarrying, agriculture, logging, squatting, fires and housing development. It is estimated that there are some 25,000 squatter households on State lands, with equal numbers on private lands (Vision 2020 Planning Committee 2003b) and as such, over 15 % of the households in Trinidad and Tobago are squatting on State lands, with a significant proportion of these lands once being forested. The State Lands (Regularisation of Tenure) Act (No. 25 of 1998) identifies 254 squatter settlements on State lands of which approximately 52 % of the total squatter settlements on State lands are located in southern Trinidad. Many of these households are located in forest reserves, with the problem being prevalent in the Tacarigua Reserve, the Valencia Reserve, and the Long Stretch Reserve in the north and in the Southern Watershed and Rochard Douglas Reserve in the south, where both agricultural and housing squatting are common. Forest clearance has made way for formal high-density housing developments and other forms of residential development, for example, in Mosquito Creek and Icacos. Recent forest loss has been recorded with the expansion of the natural gas industry and downstream petrochemical industries in southern Trinidad, namely the development of 154 ha (380 acre) Union Estate Industrial Park in La Brea; the construction of the Train IV Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) plant; and the trans-island pipeline from Guayaguayare to Point Fortin/La Brea. Further losses can be expected with the possible development of 809 ha (2000 acre) heavy industrial estate in Cap-de-Ville. The construction of roadways and the laying of cross-island pipelines in forested areas in southern Trinidad have not only resulted in deforestation but also the dissection of forest habitats, thus increasing environmental stress on fragmented and reduced species populations. The 2001/2002 State of the Environment Report noted that there are some 13,832 km length of Class One to Four roads in Trinidad and 668 km in Tobago, indicating that both islands have the highest score in respect of habitat fragmentation38.
