Chapter 5: Coasts and Oceans

5.1 Introduction As an archipelagic state, Trinidad and Tobago’s claimed marine area covers 75,000 km2 with a 370.4 km (200 nautical miles) EEZ that extends from its territorial sea baseline. The marine environment is influenced heavily by its geographic location on the continental shelf of South America. The islands are located in relatively shallow waters, and closeness to the Orinoco River’s mouth provides, among other things, a nutrient rich marine environment which supports a large fish population. The coastal environment consists of substantial areas of seagrass beds, wetlands and coral reefs, oil and gas deposits, ports and piers and coastal protection structures. Water quality is affected by sedimentation from the rivers of the South American mainland as well as those from inland Trinidad and Tobago; natural seepages from oil and gas wells as well as leaks from abandoned and operational pipelines; bilge water and oil leaks from marine vessels and wrecks; coastal works which seek to protect the coastline or reclaim land; urbanisation; and industrial effluent discharge. Wetlands of Trinidad and Tobago consist principally of freshwater swamp forests, mangrove swamps, and freshwater marshes. In all, there are 71 wetland sites covering over 25,000 ha. A detailed description of the country’s wetlands is provided in Chapter Six. Seagrass (Thalassia testudinum) beds are located in the Gulf of Paria along the north-western peninsula (with dense beds located in William’s Bay, St Peter’s Bay and Dhein’s Bay) and in Pointe a Pierre. In Tobago, seagrass beds are found in Bon Accord Lagoon, Buccoo Bay, Pigeon Point, La Guira Bay, Kilgwyn Bay, Petit Trou Lagoon and King’s Bay. These seagrass communities which provide food and safe habitat for marine life are currently extending their coverage, yet they either remain under threat or are stressed from a range of activities including coastal development, sewage, and damage from boats’ anchors and propellers. Algal growth and coral reefs are supported on the hard sea bottom in several places such as the Manzanilla Bank (running east to Darien Rock), the Drew Shallows (located between Toco and Tobago), the Emerald Shoals and Delaware Bank (located east of Toco) and other areas on the north eastern coastline of Tobago (Figure 5.1). However, much of the rest of the nation’s marine inshore sea bottom varies from mud to sand with patches of shell debris (Van Andel and Postma 1954). Well developed coral reefs are found around Tobago and Salybea, Trinidad. Coral reefs are found in Northwest Trinidad in Scotland Bay, Chacachacare and Monos Island. Sea surface temperature variations are relatively small in comparison to variations in sea surface salinity over seasonal cycles. Sea surface temperature ranges from a dry season average of 26°C, to a wet season average of 28°C, whereas average levels of salinity can range from 35 parts per thousand during the dry season to less than 15 parts per thousand (in the Gulf of Paria for instance) during the wet season. Salinity changes are associated with large volumes of fresh water input from the Orinoco River (Van Andel and Postma 1954). Trinidad and Tobago have 704 km of coastline. For the most part, the north coasts of both islands are deeply indented with a relatively large number of bays some of which are inaccessible by land because of the very high elevations and steep slopes. Trinidad’s west coast comprises unconsolidated sediments between Port of Spain and San Fernando resulting in low cliffs punctuated with beaches and swamps. The more resistant materials make up the promontories at Brighton, Point Fortin and Cedros Point on the south-west coast.
