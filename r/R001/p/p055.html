3.2.5 Response Trinidad and Tobago joined the international community in making a legal commitment to address the challenges posed by climate change. The country became a signatory to the UNFCCC in June 1992 and ratified it in June 1994. This Convention, which recognises that the global climate system is a shared resource, aims to stabilise GHG concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent human activities from influencing the climate systems. As part of this Convention, Trinidad and Tobago is required to undertake, periodically update, publish, and make available to the Conference of Parties (COP), its national inventory on GHG and its national programmes to mitigate and adapt to global climate change. In 2001, the EMA prepared the country’s Initial National Communication which describes the national GHG inventory, and provides statements on vulnerability and adaptation, as well as identification of technology needs, capacity building, and research (EMA 2001b). However, the report was prepared using 1990 data and therefore there may be a need to undertake a new GHG inventory, given the dynamic changes that have taken place in the country’s oil and natural gas sector within recent times. This new inventory would have to be supported by reliable data, and so the need to establish the relevant databases can be considered an urgent requirement. The Meteorological Services Division forms the main repository of climatic data in Trinidad and Tobago and provides useful data on local weather in both Trinidad and Tobago over the years. In addition, other agencies such as the IMA, the EMA, the WRA, the Office of Disaster Preparedness and

Management (ODPM)/National Emergency Management Agency (NEMA), Tobago Emergency Management Agency (TEMA) and UWI are currently assessing and working on various aspects of climate variability and change. However, the Meteorological Services Division along with its partner agencies may need to further collaborate in assessing climate change and ensuring that information is made available on a regular and timely basis. One of the first tasks in this collaborative effort ought to be the development of a set of indicators on climate change for Trinidad and Tobago, for which data can be collected, which could be used to measure the effects of climate variability and change. The Initial National Communication to the UNFCCC notes that “one of the most important aspects of adaptation planning to global climate change is the ability to continuously assess the vulnerability and adaptation options of various sectors with some degree of certainty”.
