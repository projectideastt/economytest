Trinidad and Tobago’s position in the market is likely to remain strong. 8.2.3 State and Trends Domestic Oil and Gas Utilisation The 2005 Annual Economic Survey Report by the Central Bank (Central Bank of Trinidad and Tobago 2005) shows production of crude oil (including condensates) averaged 144.4 thousand barrels per day in 2005 and resulted in a total annual production of 52.7 million barrels, a 27.2 % increase over the 2001 figure (Table 8.3). Natural gas production grew even faster than crude oil. Between 2001 and 2005, natural gas production and utilisation doubled, giving an average of 3,219 million cubic feet per day and 3,033 million cubic feet per day, respectively. In 2005, the LNG industry accounted for 49.2 % of total usage, followed by the ammonia industry (16.5 %), the methanol industry (12.4 %) and power generation (7.6 %). The remainder was absorbed by the iron and steel industry and other small consumers. Table 8.3 shows the production and utilisation in the oil and gas sector. Electricity Generation and Consumption Use of energy is important in improving people’s standard of living, and as such, availability of electricity is used as a measure of living conditions. At the same time, electricity generation can damage the environment, depending largely on how it is generated. More than 99 % of Trinidad and Tobago‘s electricity source is natural gas which is considered to be well suited environmentally for power generation because of its low carbon to hydrogen ratio. While the population size stabilised over the decade (1995 to 2004), electricity generation rose by about 59 % to 6.7 billion kwh, with per capita consumption climbing from 2,702.5 kwh in 1995 to 4,543.7 kwh in 2004. Industrial activities accounted for the highest electricity consumption, averaging 65 % of total electricity consumed and 2.5 times the amount consumed by residential users, the second highest consumers (Table 8.4). 8.2.4 Response Non-renewable energy use, in the form of fossil fuels, will continue to be the main engine of socio- economic growth and development in Trinidad and Tobago for as long as supply lasts and its utilisation remain viable. Therefore, the use of renewable energy sources is not likely to be given priority at this time. However, there has been a recent call by the South Chamber of Industry and Commerce and the Caribbean Association of Industry and Commerce for the development of a Common Caribbean Energy Policy (South Chamber of Industry and Commerce 2006) which should address five critical issues. Among them is the issue of the environment and energy conservation. In their argument for such an energy policy, the authors of the framework note that there should be a focus on both mitigating negative environmental impacts of the energy industry and on encouraging energy conservation, through consumer education programmes and development of appropriate renewable technology. These will be more cost efficient if undertaken at a regional level.
