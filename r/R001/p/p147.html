Chapter 7: Biodiversity 7.1 Overview The fauna and flora of Trinidad and Tobago are a natural extension of the ecology of South America. Because of its relatively small size, location and the geological relationship to the mainland, the country has an abundance and variety of biological resources, and thus a high species diversity to surface area ratio. The range of terrestrial ecosystems includes several forest types - evergreen seasonal; semi- evergreen seasonal; deciduous seasonal; littoral woodlands; lower montane rainforests; seasonal montane forests; montane rainforests; elfin woodlands; and swamp forests (including mangrove woodlands) - as well as palm swamps, marshes and savannahs (Figure 7.1). The marine ecosystems include: the water masses; mud bottom; sandy bottom; rocky shores; beaches; seagrass beds; coral reefs; and mudflats. By far, the mud bottoms dominate the near shore areas. The marine systems support a range of macro and micro biota including a large array of fish species of commercial importance. Many fish species may be found common to more than one type of sea bottom. The greatest diversity is associated with coral reef systems. One main drawback is that there is no comprehensive listing of many plant and forest species, particularly in the case of the Main Ridge, Tobago. Although the review of taxa in Trinidad and Tobago is far from complete, at latest count the terrestrial fauna for the archipelago exceeds 1,000 species and the flora, 2,500 species (Forestry Division 2005). Many marine species are important food species. These include fishes, shrimp, crabs, lobsters, and molluscs. Widespread concern continues to be expressed for the decline in numbers of some species and potential extinction of others. Manatees (Trichechus manatus), for example, were previously known to live in both the South Oropouche and Caroni drainages, but only a small herd remains extant in Nariva. The ecological status of many plant and animal species is not clear. However, what is clear is the need for Trinidad and Tobago to embark on an assessment of the status of its species for more accurate categorisation. A number of challenges face Trinidad and Tobago in its task to maintain species biodiversity and to conserve the country’s wildlife habitats. The major pressures on habitat conservation and protection are loss, fragmentation and degradation due mainly to deforestation. This ultimately affects species numbers. The issue of biodiversity and climate change is a critical one given recent predictions of rising ambient temperatures, changing rainfall patterns, and sea level rise. 7.2 Ecosystem Conservation and Protection Loss, fragmentation and degradation are the major challenges to the protection and conservation of ecosystems and this is due to a number of driving forces including deforestation, bush and forest fires, pollution and decreasing freshwater quality. Less is known about the true status of some marine habitats. Protected areas in Trinidad and Tobago represent more than 25 % of the country’s total land area. However, the status of many of these areas remains questionable.
