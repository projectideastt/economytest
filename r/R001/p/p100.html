In 2004, holders of agricultural parcels numbered 19,143 but this was a reduction from 1982 when there were 30,566 holders, a decline of 11,423 or 37.4%. In addition, agricultural land that was recently abandoned by Caroni is being returned to productive use. Thus, the 1,012 ha of abandoned paddy fields, which were once cultivated by Caroni, were returned to production in 2005 through government leases to two private operators, one of which is the Caribbean Rice Farmers Association of Trinidad and Tobago. 4.4.4 Response Government’s current policy of transforming some 8,250 ha of former Caroni’s sugar cane lands into small parcels of 1 ha size, for production of food crops and vegetables, will increase the number of parcel holders by approximately 7,000 at completion of infrastructure and distribution of leases. This may act to some extent to reverse the trend identified in the 2004 Agricultural Census. Training sessions are organised each year for farmers in the use of agro-chemicals, land preparation and fire prevention. So far, data are not readily available on the number and type of training sessions held, or the number of farmers trained by the Extension Services of the MALMR as well as the impact of these programmes on farming. The Youth Apprenticeship Programme for Agriculture is one of the programmes in place at the high school level to encourage youth to engage in farming. Information was not available at time of reporting. The State Lands Act and its recent amendment are in place to restrict illegal access on State land for agricultural and non-agricultural land uses. However, it is not enforced and appeared to have had little impact on current agricultural squatting. The LSA is engaged in the regularisation of squatters and physical upgrading of their communities. Between 2002 and 2005, 6 unauthorised housing communities were upgraded and 4 were in the process of upgrading. Additionally another 21 sites were in various stages of pre-construction action.
