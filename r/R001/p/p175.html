Environmentally Sensitive Species (ESS) The Environmentally Sensitive Species (ESS) Rules of 2001 allow the EMA to designate any species of living plants or animals that are endemic, endangered or vulnerable as ESS based on scientific evidence or international treaties to which Trinidad and Tobago is a signatory. This designation, along with the implementation of a species management plan, is geared at the full recovery of the species to the point where the protection of being listed as an ESS is not necessary. In 2004/2005, 3 species were designated as ESS, namely, the white-tailed sabrewing hummingbird (Campylopterus ensipennis), the West Indian manatee (Trichechus manatus), and the Trinidad piping guan or ‘pawi’ (Pipile pipile). Discussion and research were initiated by the EMA in 2005 regarding 5 species of marine turtles: the loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta), the leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea), the olive ridley turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea), the green turtle (Chelonia mydas), and the hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata). Recovery Plans Recovery plans are considered an important response to address the issue of declining biodiversity. One objective of the EMA’s Strategic Work Plan for the period 2003-2008 calls for the development and implementation of management/ recovery plans for 10 species identified to be designated as ESS. These include the 5 species of marine turtles mentioned above, the West Indian manatee, the white-tailed sabrewing hummingbird, the pawi, the ocelot (Leopardus pardalis) and the golden tree frog (Phyllodytes auratus). So far, recovery plans have been developed for the manatee and sea turtles. However, information on the outcomes and highlights of the implementation of these plans in the recovery of these species are limited at this time. It was expected that information available from current research on the manatee could be used to update its recovery plan, and be used in its management. The St Augustine Campus of the UWI is currently undertaking research on the white-tailed sabrewing hummingbird and the pawi. It is expected that this on-going research will serve as the basis for the development of species recovery plans.
