The islands are affected mainly by the subtropical anticyclone belt and the inter-tropical convergence zone (ITCZ), and it is the movement and location of these two systems which give the weather its marked seasonal characteristics noted in Trinidad and Tobago and the rest of the Caribbean. From January to May, drier conditions are experienced (dry season), and from June to December, wetter conditions dominate (wet season). For most years, there is a short dry spell, the Petit Carême, during September and October. It is during the wet season from June through to the end of November that there is risk of tropical storms and hurricanes. However, Trinidad and Tobago lies at the southern edge of the North Atlantic hurricane belt and this makes the islands less prone to being hit by hurricanes when compared with most other Caribbean countries. 3.2.3 Pressure The IPCC 2001 Report notes the “dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system”. Human activities have increased the atmospheric concentrations of GHGs and ozone depleting materials since the pre-industrial era. The Report notes that the atmospheric concentrations of key anthropogenic GHG (carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and tropospheric ozone) reached their highest recorded levels in the 1990s, primarily due to the combustion of fossil fuels, agriculture, deforestation, and land-use changes. The concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere has increased by some 31 % from 280 ppm to 368 ppm since the pre-industrial era, while the concentration of methane has more than doubled.
