1.3 New Approach to Reporting The 2005 ASOE seeks to provide a benchmark for continued, systematic reporting on environmental and developmental issues. Several steps have been taken in this report to lay the groundwork for future reports, and these are as follows: • A framework for reporting on the state of the environment, known as the Pressure-State-Response (PSR) Model, is introduced. This model was developed by the Organisation for Economic Co- operation and Development (OECD) in 1993, and is currently utilised by a large number of countries for international and national reporting on the environment (OECD 2003). Section 1.4 describes the PSR Model in more detail. The section also explains how the PSR model will be used in conjunction with other frameworks (such as the conceptual MA framework) in order to capture the interconnection between humans and the environment. • On the basis of the PSR Model, and through stakeholder consultations, the 2005 ASOE began the development of a national set of environmental indicators for Trinidad and Tobago. Indicators are tools that can be used to obtain information on issues of relevance to human development, thereby allowing for analyses of societal change (Segnestam 2002). In the case of assessing the state of environment, indicators can be used to collect useful information on environmental health and quality and provide better insights on: conditions and trends; impacts on any given component of the environment; the pressures acting on the environment; and outcomes of human responses to environmental change. The full list of core indicators developed is included in Annex 1. • It has been recognized however that Trinidad and Tobago will have great difficulty obtaining data in the short to medium term to utilise the full set of core indicators proposed by the OECD. A realistic approach would be for the EMA, in collaboration with stakeholders, to identify and select locally relevant and achievable indicators from the full OECD set. In this manner, a comprehensive subset of indicators capable of immediate use for ongoing monitoring and evaluation of Trinidad and Tobago’s environmental performance can be derived. Trinidad and Tobago can then develop and implement the necessary data acquisition systems to allow eventual expansion to the full set of core OECD indicators or theoretically define indicators for our unique situation. It should also be noted that national indicators are important for allowing comparisons across national boundaries, so that Trinidad and Tobago can keep abreast of its obligations to international environmental conventions (for example, UNCBD, United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification [UNCCD], and United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change [UNFCCC]). • The process for preparation of the 2005 ASOE attempted to bring together a group of relevant public sector agencies and research institutions and organisations in order to make the crucial links amongst issues and between sectors related to environment and development.
