Executive Summary

GENERAL Section 14(1) of the Environmental Management Act (Act 3 of 2000) mandates the Environmental Management Authority (EMA) to report annually on the state of the national environment. The Assessment of the State of Environment (ASOE) provides a public forum for informed discourse on the state of some aspects of the natural environment, the human actions or natural phenomena which impact negatively on the environment and actions taken to reduce or ameliorate the negative ones. This ASOE which responds to the EMA’s mandate is organised around eight chapters, of which the first two deal with the new approach taken in the presentation of its contents and the main drivers influencing environmental change. Each of the remaining six chapters deals with a theme identified as critical to the natural environment. The themes include the atmosphere; land; coasts and oceans; inland waters; biodiversity; and economy, industry and resource use. These thematic areas represent the utilisation of resources that are being impacted by human action either in their use in building human civilization or as by-products of human activities. For the most part, previous ASOEs prepared by the EMA have had a single focus or a single theme. This 2005 Report departs from this trend in that several aspects of the environment are dealt with simultaneously. This approach allows reporting of a complex topic in a more appropriate manner. It is also the base for development of a system which, over time, will enable the EMA to track changes continuously in the environment, and monitor and report nationally and internationally on these changes. The approach is therefore more consistent with the spirit of national legislation, as well as with Trinidad and Tobago’s international obligations under the several conventions on aspects of the natural environment to which it is signatory. The Report recognises the manipulation of the natural environment over time and acknowledges the well-known relationship between environmental change and socio-economic activities. It notes a list of factors or drivers identified by the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA) which aid in the destruction of the natural environment. These drivers are both direct (e.g. physical, biological and chemical drivers) and indirect (e.g. demographic, economic, socio-political, scientific and technical, as well as religious drivers). The Report focuses on the natural resource component of the environment as defined in the Environmental Management Act of 2000, but it also includes social, economic and cultural elements as important components. Thus, one of the six themes is economy and industry. The model adopted in this Report which also will be utilized in the future, is known as the Pressure–State–Response (PSR) Model which is used by a large number of countries for national and international reporting on the natural environment. The usefulness of the PSR Model for Trinidad and Tobago lies in the fact that it reflects the key environmental issues which are of concern to stakeholder agencies. It also reflects indicators on the key issues which pose the greatest risk to the environment. Perhaps more importantly, many of the key issues such as pollution, flooding, urbanisation, and forest fires are widely known and understood nationally. The model will allow for more detailed examination of these as they affect the natural environment and the natural resources of the two islands. The issues were derived from the Environmental Committee with responsibility for preparation of the ASOE, interviews with representatives of stakeholder agencies and a review of plans and environmental reports. Relevant issues were identified for each thematic area and guided the Report’s preparation.
