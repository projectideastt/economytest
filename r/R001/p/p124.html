In a recent survey of fishing practices on the south coast of Trinidad (IMA 1999), fisherfolk generally indicated that there was a reduction of fish stock in the Columbus Channel and suggested that one of the reasons for this was the destructive practice of members of their fraternity who leave sections of mono filament nets in the water and on reefs. This practice causes fish to be ensnared and killed long after the sections of nets have been discarded because of damages sustained from marine vessels. The marine environment is a multiple use zone providing a range of services and products to different users. Non-complementary uses are engendering increasing conflicts among fishermen, recreational visitors, operators of marine transport vessels and oil and gas miners. These conflicts will need to be mediated otherwise the marine environment will become increasingly difficult for fishermen and recreational users. 5.4.3 Condition and Trends According to the IMA, the nation had an annual average fish catch of between 7,000 and 10,000 tonnes (IMA 1999). However, based on estimates of total landings by fleet by the Fisheries Division, total commercial fish landings ranged from 12,380 tonnes in 1996 to 17,966 tonnes in 2002. Table 5.6 shows a breakdown by type of fishing fleet for the five year period 2000 to 2004. It should be noted though that the data on fish catch provided by the Fisheries Division are essentially estimates for the artisanal, industrial, and semi-industrial fleets in Trinidad, and for Tobago. In the case of Tobago, the Division noted that a crude estimate for 1991 was used. Nonetheless, the FAO in its 1998 Country Profile report for Trinidad and Tobago noted that the data for the artisanal fishermen were generally reliable. The information and data on landings adequately cover the artisanal and inshore fisheries, and can be considered as being relatively dependable. However, it is somewhat more difficult to obtain information and data for the industrial and semi-industrial fleet, since these operations do not currently allow for easy monitoring. In addition, there is some reluctance by operators to divulge the required information. A similar lack of information makes an assessment of the state of inland/swamp fisheries for crabs, oysters and cascadura impossible. 5.4.4 Response The Central Government provides a range of subsidies to the fisheries industry. They include subsidies on fuels for fishing vessels and for purchase of new (multi-purpose offshore) and replacement of (inshore) old vessels as well as exemptions of duty and tax on engines and spare parts, boats and navigation aids.
