Industrial waste is discharged into waterways primarily from manufacturing and agricultural enterprises in the East-West Corridor and the Southern Corridor including the industrial estates of central Trinidad. Given the growth in the manufacturing sector, the volume of and number of effluent sources have increased. Increased concentrations of heavy metals have been found in several rivers. In Tobago, the growth of the tourism sector, which is heavily concentrated in the south–west, makes tremendous demand on the water resources and competes with other users, particularly domestic users, for an increasingly scarce supply. Sewerage treatment plants, some of which are non-functional, are sending raw or almost raw sewage into many of the nation’s waterways, and the ubiquitous pit latrine, some of which are constructed in inappropriate soils, often leak into nearby waterways. These actions decrease water quality in the riverine environment, making them unsafe for recreation and irrigation. Urban development is proceeding apace with expansion of housing and other buildings close to or on river banks. Even some of the upper reaches of the watersheds are themselves being subjected to degradation through deforestation and landslides. Moreover, drainage and river reserves established by the Drainage Division of the Ministry of Works are being breached by developers while makeshift bridges are placed across some of the rivers to provide access for residents and their vehicles. Not only is water quality decreasing as a consequence of pollution, but quantity and availability continue to be a challenge. The demand for water is increasing with expansion of the nation’s population and their concentrations in urban centres as well as expansion of the industrial base. In the case of Tobago, the hotel and resort sector has had tremendous growth and this has been reflected in that sector’s increasing demand for potable water. Solid waste, including furniture, kitchen and laundry appliances and hulks of vehicles are thrown into gullies and rivers clogging drainage channels and changing the rate of flow of some waterways.
