2.2.3 Poverty While Trinidad and Tobago has made significant strides in socio-economic development, a considerable proportion of its population live below the poverty line. A 2004 report put the level of poverty in Trinidad and Tobago at 16.7% of the population, while the level of indigent poverty was at 1.2% of the population. The poverty line was defined as TT$665 per month, per capita (Inter-American Development Bank 2004a). Although not all economically poor communities engage in activities which lead to environmental degradation, some low income groups lack the means and resources to provide for some of their basic needs and are known to resort to the unsustainable exploitation of natural resources. In Trinidad and Tobago, unauthorised occupation of land is not restricted to the landless and poor. However, historical patterns of lack of access to affordable land and housing by some low income groups has exacerbated the problem of squatting and has generated a myriad of environmental problems including: poor sanitary conditions; inadequate liquid and solid waste disposal; and deficient drainage systems and road networks. For example, the IADB report showed that about 62% of the lowest quintile (the poor) was dependent on pit latrines while appropriately 72% of the indigent was dependent on pit latrines (Ibid.). 2.2.4 Approach to Development Over the last decade or so Trinidad and Tobago has pursued a path of industrialisation focused on gas-based heavy industry located for the most part on the western coast of Trinidad. The first industrial complex at Point Lisas is currently being used as the blueprint for further industrial development and the Government is in the lead, as with Point Lisas, in acquiring mostly foreign-based partners to locate their plants in Trinidad. With their large demand for land, water and electrical energy, some of these new industries have the potential to negatively impact the natural environment, unless carefully planned and monitored. Already the concerns for relocation of residents, displacement of fisher-folk and decline in coastal water quality are being expressed in many quarters. Thus, the competition for scarce space in a SIDS such as Trinidad and Tobago, coupled with small margins for environmental errors, will require monitoring programmes that offer very early warnings to vigilant environmental groups and authorities to avert disaster.
