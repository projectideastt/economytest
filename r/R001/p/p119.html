Both cruise ships and yacht arrivals increased over the period 2004 to 2005 for Trinidad and Tobago. The number of cruise ship arrivals increased from 72 in the 2004/2005 period to 89 in the 2005/2006 period with the majority (55) berthing in Tobago in the 2005/2006 period. 5.3.2 Pressure The marine transportation sector is a source of oil leaks, discharge of ballast water and dumping of waste into the environment. While there has been no major dislocation of shipping routes into and out of the nation’s ports, vessel movements in some parts of the Gulf of Paria such as Chaguaramas are becoming more chaotic as pleasure crafts and supply boats vie for space for manoeuvring and mooring. There is an increase in the number of ship movements both within the Gulf of Paria and around the two islands, and ship pilots have expressed concern about the absence of a ship movement monitoring system15. Unfortunately, data on ship movement by type, draft, cargo, etc are not readily available. Discharge of bilge water has been known to be one source of introduction of non-native species of flora and fauna into local marine environments, and some of these foreign species pose a threat to ecosystems and the survival of native species. Marine traffic generated by the oil and gas industry is often responsible for damaging fishing gear especially on the south and east coasts of Trinidad. The marine traffic scenario is one of an increase in the numbers and types of vessels using the marine waters around Trinidad and Tobago. Associated with these increases is the potential for accidents, and particularly collision of oil and gas tankers. The problem may be of greater importance within the Gulf of Paria where there are tremendous ship movements and no modern marine traffic control mechanisms as mentioned earlier. 5.3.3 Condition and Trends Marine transportation is likely to increase not only within the Gulf of Paria but around the nation’s coastal waters with expansion in exploration and development of rigs and platforms. Moreover, traffic in the Gulf of Paria is expected sometime in the future to incorporate water taxis operating from San Fernando to Port of Spain as an alternative to road transport. Pollution from a growing fleet of vessels is also likely to increase. No data were available on vessel strength by fleet and port of call. These data will need to be systematically collected. 5.3.4 Response Apart from the local legislation already cited, the Government has prepared a plan for modernising ports and port activities in Trinidad and Tobago. This draft Port Plan is currently being studied by stakeholders.
