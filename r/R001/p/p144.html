Most wetlands are located in Trinidad, and the largest proportion of wetlands comprise of those that are less than one hectare in size. The two largest wetlands, which extend over 14,600 ha combined, are the Caroni and Nariva. Both are internationally well known and along with the Buccoo Reef/Bon Accord Lagoon, are protected areas. Their sizes and designation as Ramsar Sites are provided in Chapter Seven. 6.4.2 Pressure More than 50% of the nation’s original wetlands have been lost over the years and the current stock is under severe pressure from over exploitation36 from a variety of sources. For the most part, wetlands are perceived as unimportant and without value. They are therefore seen by many as appropriate areas for filling and land reclamation, garbage dumps and agriculture. The remaining wetlands are directly impacted by rice farming in places like Fishing Pond, South Oropouche and Nariva. Along the west coast of Trinidad, land reclamation and consequential urban development (residential, commercial and industrial) have occurred at Westmoorings, Invaders Bay and Sea Lots in the north-west; Point Lisas in Couva in central; and Atlantic LNG in Point Fortin in the south. Apart from the reduction in the sizes of the wetland areas, there has been pollution of the areas because of the industrial and commercial activities associated with the built developments in close proximity to the wetlands, as well as from land-based sources which may originate miles away from the wetland. Agro-chemicals, animal waste from pig and poultry farms, silt from mining of aggregate, and industrial effluent discharged from further up the river basin find their way into the rivers and then into the wetlands and particularly the coastal swamps. These pollutants including heavy metals and faecal coliforms, degrade the wetland ecosystem and reduce the ability to execute their functions and provide their ecosystem services.
