It is estimated that an average of 1 kg of waste is generated per capita per day, ranging from 0.55 kg per capita per day in the rural areas to 1.75 kg per capita per day for a highly commercialised and industrialised area such as Port of Spain (Vision 2020 Planning Committee 2003a). Given a population of 1.3 million people, the average daily total waste should be in the order of 1.56 million kg or roughly 1,560 tonnes. Although the Beetham area had long been used for dumping of solid waste, the landfill facility, which began accepting waste in 1970, receives about 721.8 tonnes of waste each day, or little less than half of the daily tonnage of municipal solid waste delivered to the public landfills in Trinidad. The difference in projected generation of garbage and that reaching the three major landfills is 163 tonnes per day. Thus, the remaining landfills should be receiving the difference. However, there are no data for quantity of solid waste being received at the remaining six formal dump sites. Moreover, anecdotal evidence suggests that much of the remaining garbage is either not reaching the dump sites or much more garbage is being generated daily than previously reported. The quantity of waste entering the Beetham facility has almost doubled in the last 10 years (Table 4.20). A 2004 Inter-American Development Bank study shows the components of municipality solid waste reaching the official landfills in Trinidad (Table 4.21). These figures come out from studies done as early as 1995. Undoubtedly the composition of solid waste reaching the landfill would have changed over the years, with greater consumption of goods in plastic and expanded polystyrene (Styrotex™) packaging and containers. Today more than 50% of the waste reaching the landfill could be recycled9. A similar situation is noted at the Forres Park and Guanapo Landfills (Table 4.22). The quantity of solid waste entering these facilities has increased over the years with expansion of residential areas and increased industrial activities in the regions which these landfills serve. A 1989 study of the solid waste generation and disposal situation in Tobago reported that about 15 tonnes of solid waste had to be disposed of on a daily basis (Akili 2005). Some 18 years later the volume of garbage has increased considerably with growth in per capita incomes, altered patterns of consumption, and the rapid development of the tourism sector. There are no data which record Tobago’s daily per capita generation of waste. Over the years, however, it is noted that the quality and composition of the waste have also changed significantly, containing less biodegradable materials. Much less information is available for the other disposal sites but what is clear is the lower level treatment of the waste received at these sites. No consistent compaction or cover is provided nor is venting pursued. These disposal sites operate less as sanitary landfills and more like garbage dumps with grave implications for public health. Hazardous Waste Management and Disposal As one of the most diversified and industrialised economies in the English-speaking Caribbean, Trinidad and Tobago has a wide variety of manufacturing industries which include wood preserving and wood products, textiles, leather/tanning, printing, plastics, polyvinyl chloride (PVC), chlorine, sulphuric acid, battery manufacturing, metal fabrication, electrical machinery and components, metal fabrication and shipbuilding/repair. There are 19 industrial estates in the country of which the largest is Point Lisas with 85 tenants (Weston 2004). In addition, there are 4 major hospitals, 20 private clinics and 40 medical laboratories. Coupled with the ever-increasing vehicle population, the agricultural sector and domestic activities, the combined impact is the production of a large amount of hazardous waste.
