Sea water intrusion, for example, has already impacted on the biodiversity of the South Oropouche river system. The Initial National Communication for the UNFCCC considers the Caroni Basin the most vulnerable to the impacts of climate change and sea level rise. This is because it is the most densely populated area of the country and also has a concentration of critical biodiversity which encompasses from the forested Northern Range to the coastal mangrove and swamp fringes. Most of the country’s important surface and groundwater resources are found in this zone. Current inappropriate land management practices are already putting this region under threat of deforestation, annual fires, perennial flooding, siltation of water courses and overland and freshwater pollution and loss of biodiversity. Any change in climate is likely to further exacerbate the situation. 3.3.2 Pressure With more than 40 % of the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) derived from the energy sector, both the economy and lifestyles of the people of Trinidad and Tobago are driven primarily on the consumption of the country’s oil and natural gas reserves. The use of these resources extends from primary production, transportation, power generation, refining and processing and downstream petrochemical production. This reliance on the energy sector is projected to continue in the long term, as the country’s future development is based on a further deepening and strengthening of the industrialisation process. As of 2005, there were 27 energy-based plants and this number would increase substantially over the next 5 to 10 years with the proposed establishment of other energy-based industries. The recent upsurge in activities in the oil and natural gas sector can only spell increased GHG emissions. Emissions of GHG due to road transportation are substantial and are likely to rise with increasing numbers of vehicular traffic. Since the mid 1990s, the number of motor vehicles on register has almost doubled to just over 400,000 (Figure 3.12). The country’s recent windfall and affluence from the energy industry is likely to continue pushing the population’s expectations for owning cars and other motor vehicles. At present, the number of registered vehicles per capita stands at 0.312 as compared with 0.117 in 1995. With the main form of passenger transportation in Trinidad and Tobago being private vehicles (about 69 % of all registered motor vehicles and 0.204 vehicles per capita in 2004), the control of total vehicle numbers on the whole, and more specifically the private vehicle numbers, will continue to be a challenge for the nation’s policy makers.
