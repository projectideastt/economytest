Marine Ecosystems and Species While Sahara dust may bring minerals and nutrients that may change the chemical composition of the ocean to the benefit of some species, there is mounting evidence to suggest that dust storms may be accelerating the loss of coral reefs in the Caribbean. Preliminary work by the USGS and others are showing that Sahara dust can bring micro-organisms and chemical pollutants, such as a fungus known to cause a coral-reef-killing disease, across the Atlantic to the Caribbean. Some also speculate that the increased deposit of iron in the marine waters could increase the populations of harmful species of algae, such as those that cause red tides. The increased frequency of Sahara dust events could mean increased risks to species and the ecological balance of marine environment. 7.4.3 Response Chapter Three also examined the initiatives taking place to address the issue of climate change and therefore would be of relevance to this Section. However, limited feedback was received on the initiatives which address specifically the issue of Biodiversity and Climate Change. Socio-economic development requires that Trinidad and Tobago take a multi-sectoral approach which includes addressing the urgent issue of climate variability and change. This requires the adaptation of initiatives which would continuously assess and monitor vulnerabilities to climate change. Therefore, the collection of information and its translation into user friendly formats are important for easy interpretation by all users. The preparation of a sustainable land use plan for Trinidad and Tobago is a great opportunity for the country to take the critical issue of climate change on board during a comprehensive national development process. It is also important to develop and implement research programmes geared at assessing the impacts of climate change on local biodiversity.
