The number of fires and total areas burnt increase during the years of harsh dry seasons when drought conditions render both forested and non-forested lands more vulnerable to fires. Singh (2001) reported that 41 ha (100 acres) of natural forested land burn annually. However, this figure could be closer to 82 to 123 ha (200 to 300 acres), with larger acreage lost during severe dry seasons. The most severe burning was recorded during the drought of 1987, when it is estimated that over 21,000 ha were burnt, with over 4,100 ha estimated to be natural forests. Evidence of this damage is still observed in many areas such as Valencia, where many trees were killed and toppled by fire and natural regeneration has been quite variable. Recovery also has been set back by subsequent fires. Drought-like conditions were again noted in subsequent years with dire consequences. The severity of the 2003 fire season resulted in an increase in the number of areas as well as the total area burnt (Singh 2003). Yet, when compared with other years of similar rainfall, fire incidence was relatively low, most likely on account of better monitoring and enforcement. Large areas were burnt during 1998, which was recorded globally as an El Niño year. Elsewhere, indiscriminate disposal of solid and hazardous waste is threatening the environment and specifically the underground water sources since leachate management is not a widespread practice. Of the seven well known disposal sites in Trinidad and Tobago, only three practice leachate management and such management is absent among unofficial dump sites. The extent of toxicity leaching into the soils and the water table is not known and is not routinely monitored. 6.3.3 Current State and Trends The country’s groundwater potential is estimated at 611 mcm per year (545 mcm per year for Trinidad and 66 mcm for Tobago). Groundwater abstraction for 2000 was recorded as 82 mcm per year (77 mcm per year for Trinidad and 4 mcm for Tobago; Table 6.5).
