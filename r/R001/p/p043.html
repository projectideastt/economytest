The Earth’s atmosphere consists of a complex system of gases and airborne solid and liquid particles. According to the National Atmospheric and Oceanographic Administration (NOAA), about 99.96 % of dry atmosphere consists of three gases, namely nitrogen (8.08 %), oxygen (20.95 %) and argon (0.93 %). The remaining proportion (0.04 %) consists of carbon dioxide (0.035 %) and traces of other gases (NOAA 2006). Finally, water vapour usually exists in varying amounts (up to 7 %). The troposphere, the most dense and closest layer to the Earth’s surface, is the layer where people, plants and animals live, and where emissions from anthropogenic and natural sources are first discharged. It is also the region where almost all weather occurs. The next layer is the stratosphere which contains ozone which blocks large amounts of solar ultraviolet (UV) radiation from entering the troposphere and thus protects Earth and its inhabitants from its harmful effects. The other outer layers include the mesosphere, the thermosphere, and the exosphere.

The atmosphere, particularly the troposphere and stratosphere, plays a central role in maintaining environmental conditions on Earth and sustaining life as we know it. However, within recent times it has become clear that humans are significantly influencing the chemical composition of the troposphere and stratosphere in ways in which can affect the dynamic workings of the atmosphere and negatively impact humankind. In recent times, global issues such as climate variability and change, enhanced greenhouse effect and stratospheric ozone depletion have become important to the development of Trinidad and Tobago, particularly in light of the fact that we can be classified as a SIDS, which makes us extremely vulnerable to vagaries of climate change, sea level rise and the impacts of natural disasters.
