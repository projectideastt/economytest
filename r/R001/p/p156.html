Although an inventory of the Nation’s wetlands has not been completed, some 71 sites have been identified to date, which are thought to contain over 25,000 ha of swamps, marshes and ponds. The Nariva Swamp on the east coast and Caroni Swamp on the west coast are the two largest wetlands in Trinidad with a combined area of some 15,632 ha. These wetlands support a variety of plant and animal life. The coral reef systems are more diverse in Tobago when compared with Trinidad, with the Buccoo Reef, Speyside Reef and Man-o-War Reef in Tobago being the most popular and impressive (Figure 7.3). Table 7.4 also summaries the state of the country’s coral reefs and seagrass communities.

7.2.3 Responses Protected Areas System In an effort to conserve and protect the country’s biodiversity, there are 36 forest reserves, 13 wildlife sanctuaries, 1 national park, 3 beaches where entry is seasonally restricted (i.e., during the sea turtle nesting season) and 1 marine protected area (MPUE 2009). However the proportion of ecosystems represented in the various protected areas is not readily available and therefore should be documented. The forest reserves, first established during the 1920s to 1960s under the Forests Act (Chapter 66:01) were geared primarily at managing the forest resources for production forestry. Howe ver, the shift towards sustainable management and conservation of forest resources came with the recognition that existing flora and fauna were intertwined in the lives of humans, and the need for increased public awareness and participation in the management of the country’s forests. One of the first acts in changing public behaviour towards the forests was declaring a moratorium in the 1990s on timber harvesting on State lands in both Trinidad and Tobago. Nowadays, trees are usually harvested as a mechanism for improving forest health, although there are reports of illegal logging activities within some of the forest reserves.
