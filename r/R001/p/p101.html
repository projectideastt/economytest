4.5 Pollution 4.5.1 Description Waste generation from the urban centres coupled with dumping of waste into rivers, wetlands and other water bodies, surface water runoff from agricultural areas, erosion and landslides and soil removal from de-vegetated areas (particularly from hillsides by water), all contribute to pollution of the land and water bodies. For the most part, plastics have become a major packaging/container material, superseding paper and glass in some industries, and its presence as a waste product is pervasive. Plastic containers of all kinds make up a significant percentage of the solid waste generated in Trinidad and Tobago and contribute to pollution of the country’s land, water and marine resources. Solid Waste Management and Disposal Solid waste generation has escalated in Trinidad and Tobago over the past few decades as a result of interacting forces such as population growth and urban expansion, accelerated industrialisation, increasing commercialism, unsustainable production and consumption patterns (Vision 2020 Planning Committee 2003a). Thus both the type and quantity of the solid waste reaching the disposal sites have changed, with greater production and consumption of commercial, industrial and household waste. The tremendous gain made by the Chase-Charlie-Away anti-litter campaign and Nag-Charlie anti-litter campaign of the 1980s in raising awareness to the issue of waste management and littering has long since been eroded, taking the country full circle to the pre-Charlie days when there was significant land degradation resulting from ineffective waste management and disposal practices. There are nine solid waste disposal sites in Trinidad and Tobago. The three major public landfill sites are located in Trinidad, namely the Beetham, which serves the northwest region, the Guanapo which serves Northeast Trinidad, and Forres Park which serves the central and southern regions. Three other active disposal sites are organised and controlled by the regional corporations, mainly as a result of the absence of legislation governing the establishment and operation of landfills and disposal sites (Pan American Health Organisation/World Health Organisation 1998). The THA operates the only disposal site on Tobago and it is located at Studley Park. Finally there is one privately operated waste disposal facility at Guapo, Point Fortin which receives waste from the Point Fortin Borough and the Municipality of Siparia.
