This Draft Quarry Policy is still under review. Based on the Draft Quarry Policy, current operators (i.e. those operating during the period January 2001 to December 31, 2005), were being offered one year leases during which time they are expected to meet new requirements for the operations of quarries. The new requirements include among others, submission of an approved survey plan of the licensed area, payment of all outstanding royalties, a mine design plan, a restoration plan, an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) report, a CEC and an Environmental Management Plan. A Bill to amend the State Lands Act was submitted to Parliament to prevent illegal mining on State lands. When it becomes law, the State would be in a position to deal effectively with illegal quarrying on State lands with sanctions including the closure of illegal quarries and prosecution of illegal operators. Private Sector Initiatives Based on the inability of the local industry to supply aggregate to the country, several contractors are currently importing aggregate. However, the total quantity of aggregate being imported is not currently known. Many of the major oil companies operating in Trinidad and Tobago have Health, Safety and the Environment (HSE) programmes which are intended, among others, to protect employee health and safety, as well as environmental health; reduce hazards and risks to employees and the natural environment: and comply with relevant legislation. 4.4 Agriculture 4.4.1 Description Agriculture has been practiced in Trinidad and Tobago over many centuries by the native inhabitants, by slave labour on European-owned plantations and subsequently by indentured labour and free workers for private companies in which ownership shifted from foreign to State-owned companies. As a result, both the type of produce and purpose of production were altered. Shifting cultivation of indigenous crops such as manioc (cassava) and maize on relatively small plots for domestic consumption was supplanted by new crops such as sugar cane, cocoa, coconut, coffee, and cotton on large estates for export. However, cultivation of local crops for domestic consumption was also continued to ensure a cheap supply of local food for the labour force.
