They are located at Otaheite, Orange Valley, Claxton Bay and Port of Spain. 5.5 Coastal Erosion 5.5.1 Description The impact of coastal erosion on Trinidad and Tobago’s coastline varies from location to location and with spatial changes in geomorphology and exposure to open sea oceanographic conditions. In some areas the impacts are easily visible and threaten important infrastructure and property, for example: along the south Western coastline of Trinidad at Guapo and Irois Bays; along the eastern coastline of Trinidad at Manzanilla Bay and Mayaro Bay; and along parts of the southern coast of Tobago at Rockley Bay. In some instances coastal erosion may be part of larger geomorphological changes associated with seasonal high energy conditions (for example at Mayaro and Manzanilla). At other locations the coastal change is more abrupt and related to the passage of storms and hurricanes (for example on the southern coastline of Tobago). In yet other instances, the phenomena are more complex and encouraged by poor or poorly managed coastal construction activity and practices. In recent times coastal development activities, including ports, land reclamation and marine dredging along Trinidad’s south western peninsula have generated significant concern in the face of perceived exacerbation of coastal erosion by coastal residents. At any rate, systematic monitoring of the coastline on a national scale is restricted to one limited programme by the IMA and other local efforts that are parts of short term projects to support environmental impact studies. The reality of Trinidad and Tobago is that it continues to rely heavily on coastal construction as part of its larger development scheme. The Gulf of Paria in particular remains attractive to port construction and port operations as well as significant coastal engineering works to support Trinidad’s strong industrial position. While the challenges of managing the risks associated with high energy storm events will always be difficult to mitigate, the threat of coastal erosion as a result of poor coastal development is only likely to add to the problem of erosion.
