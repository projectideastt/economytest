The draft Air Pollution Rules of 2001 provide a basis for protecting public health from the adverse effects of air pollution, and for eliminating, or reducing to a minimum, those air pollutants that are known to be or are likely to be, hazardous to human health and well-being and the environment. These Rules provide a mechanism for the establishment of a register of air pollution facilities and sources, and a permitting system for the release of air pollutants. A national ambient air quality monitoring system will need to be developed and implemented so as to determine the quality of the ambient air in various key locations in Trinidad and Tobago, the extent of air pollution, the significant sources and types of pollutants, and the characterisation or description of air pollution. 3.5 Stratospheric Ozone Depletion 3.5.1 Background Ozone, though considered a pollutant in the lower atmosphere (troposphere), occurs naturally in the stratosphere, forming a protective layer against harmful UV radiation from the sun. Constantly being produced and destroyed in the stratosphere, ozone concentrations across the globe remains constantly in equilibrium, except above the poles where concentrations rise each summer and decline during the winter months. This seasonal variation is especially pronounced in the Antarctic. It was discovered in the 1970s that the natural order of this naturally occurring layer was threatened by the increased use and release of various human- produced substances, such as chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), methyl bromide and halon. In the presence of solar UV rays, these substances react to destroy ozone molecules. This reaction is pronounced at the North and South Poles in winter months, which adds to the seasonal dips observed in ozone concentration. In the Antarctic, the arrival of spring serves to worsen the situation such that an ‘ozone hole’ develops as levels become low and the layer ‘thins’. Depletion of the stratospheric ozone layer reduces its protective function by exposing humans and the environment to adverse effects of increased solar UV radiation. Human health impacts include the increased incidence of skin cancers, cataracts, blindness, and damage to the immune system. Solar UV radiation can disrupt terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems and biogeochemical cycles, and adversely affect plant life. The Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer was adopted in 1987 to limit the production and phase-out the use of ODS and related substances. Evidence suggests that international efforts to reduce the release of ODS appear to be working. Research has shown that between 1979 and 1997 ozone diminished at a rate of 8 % each decade in the upper stratosphere. However, between 1997 and 2002, the rate of depletion slowed to a projected average of just 4 % per decade (National Geographic Society 2003). It is predicted that the ozone layer will be back to its pre- industrial condition within another 50 years. 3.5.2 Pressure Many CFCs and other ODS, such as methyl bromide and halons, are very stable, with long half lives and thus remain in the atmosphere interacting with the ozone layer for a considerable period of time. One CFC molecule has the potential to destroy as many as 10,000 ozone molecules. The impact of certain ODS on the ozone layer is measured as their ozone depletion potential (ODP). Different substances have different ODPs.
