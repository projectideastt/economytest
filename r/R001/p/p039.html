2.2.5 Incompatible Land and Marine Uses The intrinsic characteristics of renewable natural resources are those properties which allow them to regenerate. Where the use to which the resource is put disrupts or destroys its intrinsic

qualities, degradation occurs. Incompatible land and marine uses are not sustainable because the resources associated with these ecosystems are unable to regenerate themselves. The examples of inappropriate uses in Trinidad and Tobago are many, ranging from: inappropriate land clearance methods which leave landscapes denuded; the construction of large houses and conducting agricultural production on steep hillsides without the use of suitable soil conservation practices and guidelines; the proliferation of illegal waste dump sites around the country; and squatting in forested areas. 2.2.6 Inadequate Institutional Capacity Experience has shown that where there is insufficient or limited institutional capacity to safeguard against unsustainable practices and behaviours, environmental degradation follows. Environmental improvement requires an alignment of legislation with the country’s principles of environmental sustainability, as well as the effective implementation and strengthening of capacity of environmental agencies, including rationalisation and improving inter-institutional collaboration. The natural resources and environment of Trinidad and Tobago are governed by some 40 pieces of legislation and managed by about 28 State agencies, with the EMA playing a regulatory and coordinating role. However, this arrangement has had limited success in meeting the environmental degradation challenge. Instead the sector is currently characterised by deficiencies in its institutional structure and capacity. 2.2.7 Poor Legislation The absence or inappropriateness of legislation limits punitive measures to preventive action, or cessation of those activities that can lead to environmental degradation. While it is recognised that regulations alone cannot protect all aspects of the environment, there is need for legal regulatory efficacy. Some legislative frameworks for various aspects of environmental management are ineffective and the penalties for non-compliance are not stringent enough to act as deterrents.
