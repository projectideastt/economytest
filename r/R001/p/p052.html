Though the occurrence of El Niño and La Niña has only been brought to the forefront as global phenomena within the last 30 years, geologic and archaeological evidence suggest that El Niño has been around for thousands of years. El Niño involves the warming of some sections of the Pacific Ocean, large scale variations in atmospheric pressure between the Pacific and Indian Oceans, and weakening or reversing of the east-west trade winds. This large-scale abnormal warming of the surface of the eastern and central Pacific Ocean is known to occur every 2 to 7 years, with varying degrees of intensity and duration. It is associated with significant changes in climate patterns all over the world, causing droughts and floods in parts of the Americas, Africa, Asia and the South Pacific. El Niño was recorded worldwide recently during 1981/1982, 1987/1988, 1997/1998 and 2002/2003. Among the more recent El Niño episodes, the 1981/1982 and 1997/1998 were the most severe and had profound effects on the world economy. In contrast, there is an abnormal cooling in the eastern Pacific during a La Niña event, which produces conditions opposite of those generated by El Niño. The effects of La Niña are most pronounced from December to March. Analysis of over 100 years of historical climate data shows that El Niño and La Niña are linked statistically to worldwide abnormal patterns of rainfall, temperature, and storminess. The Caribbean is no exception to these anomalies in climate. During El Niño years tropical cyclone activity in the Caribbean is drastically suppressed. During intense El Niño years, Trinidad and Tobago, like the rest of the southern Caribbean, experiences deficit rainfall. The reverse is true in La Niña years. However, annual rainfall totals may not reflect the impacts of El Niño, since the typical period for El Niño conditions starts in the middle of one year and ends in the middle of the following year. This abnormal dry period associated with El Niño is usually followed by an exceptionally wet rainy season. Figure 3.8 shows the variation from average annual rainfall for the last 30 years for Trinidad and Tobago. The 1997/98 El Niño, which was noted worldwide as the second strongest in the past century, did not reflect the significant changes in rainfall amount recorded at the Piarco Meteorological Station, though many remember the 1998 fire season to be an aggressive one (Figure 3.9). This suggests that the relationship between this phenomenon and the local weather needs to be studied in greater detail.
