The East-West Corridor in the capital region which extends from Carenage in the west to Arima in the east is the country’s major urban centre, and it has grown in population, size and complexity. In 1970, the capital region accommodated some 443,100 or 44% of the national population. In 1975 some 691,176 or approximately 62% of the national population lived in predominantly urban areas and the capital region accounted for approximately 490,512 or some 70% (TCPD 1984)7. The urban population continued to grow over the last 30 years with the development of new residential, commercial and industrial areas in the existing urban centres as well as new ones. Couva/Point Lisas, Maloney, Canaan/Bon Accord have all been significantly populated principally from new residential subdivisions and internal migration. However, there are no current data on urban populations by development planning regions. The Population Division of the United Nations estimates the urbanisation rate of Trinidad and Tobago to be 75% of the total population (Population Division of the United Nations Secretariat 2002). The 2000 Census of Population and Housing has identified the five main urban centres as the two cities of Port of Spain and San Fernando, and the three Boroughs of Arima, Point Fortin and Chaguanas. These urban centres had a combined urban population of 223,217 (Table 4.2) but this number excluded the built up areas which are contiguous to these centres and function as integral parts of their social and economic activities. When the urban areas are described as Greater Port of Spain, San Fernando sub- region, Greater Arima and Point Fortin including Cap De Ville, the urban population expands dramatically.
