The section on species protection considers the vulnerability of local flora and fauna to the conditions in Trinidad and Tobago and reports on the over-exploitation of certain species, including some timber species, tree ferns, orchids, freshwater fish species, prawns, marine demersal fish species, sea turtles, cage bird species, and game animals, such as deer, lappe, quenk, ducks and other waterfowl. Responses to these pressures include the declaration of the 13 wildlife sanctuaries, which prohibits hunting in these ‘sacred’ areas; the designation of Environmentally Sensitive Species (ESS); the development and implementation of recovery plans for ESS; and initiatives undertaken by State agencies, non-governmental organisations and community-based organisations in Northeast and Southeast Trinidad and in Tobago in managing the country’s biodiversity. The issue of biodiversity and climate change is a critical one given recent predictions of rising ambient air and water body temperatures, changing rainfall patterns, and sea level rise. The chapter summarises climate change predictions and observations in Trinidad and Tobago and proposes how such changes are likely to affect terrestrial, coastal and marine ecosystems and species. There is need for research and for monitoring the impact of climate change on the country’s ecosystems and biological resources. Economy, Industry, Resource Use The natural resources of Trinidad and Tobago have been and continue to be the foundation for its socio-economic development. The exploitation of oil and natural gas resources, and moreso natural gas, drives the economy. Coastal and marine resources are important for both the fishing and tourism industries, particularly to the economy of Tobago where both are the mainstay. Urban consumption of natural resources is also considerable. The official government target of 10,000 additional housing units annually has consequences for increased demand for land space, the densification of existing neighbourhoods, and the expansion into green areas. Domestic users account for the largest freshwater withdrawals, and total electricity use is also high. The chapter focuses on three issues, namely energy use, water use, and road travel and transportation. The energy sector accounted for 40.5 % of total GDP in 2004 and 41.2 % in 2005 with an overall projected growth of 20.6 % in the sector. However, as the country’s economic future remains entrenched in the energy sector and the number of proposed energy-based industries and electricity generation increase, the pressure on the oil and natural gas reserves can be tremendous. In 2004, oil and gas reserves stood at 756 million proven barrels of oil and 18.8 trillion cubic feet of proven gas reserves, respectively. Already Trinidad and Tobago is among the top ten per capita producers of carbon dioxide emissions in the world, although in terms of absolute carbon emissions, the country produces less than 1 % of the global total. There has been a recent call by the South Chamber of Industry and Commerce and the Caribbean Association of Industry and Commerce for the development of a Common Caribbean Energy Policy which focuses on mitigating the negative environmental impacts of the energy industry and on encouraging energy conservation. The availability of freshwater in Trinidad and Tobago is affected by the occurrence and distribution of rainfall as well as the occurrence, movement and quality of surface water and groundwater. As a water-rich country, Trinidad produces some 4.005 billion cubic metres/year, while Tobago produces about 0.206 billion cubic metres/year. The actual abstractions of surface and groundwater for Trinidad and Tobago in 2000 were 278 million cubic metres/year and 81.1 million cubic metres/year respectively. The main pressures on water use reported in this chapter are the high demand for water for domestic and industrial uses, water wastage and pollution. One response to the water shortage problem is the installation and use of high-volume water storage tanks by householders and businesses. In addition, the Water and Sewerage Authority improved and upgraded the water infrastructure and reduced unaccounted for water in many areas to increase the water supply to the population.
