Unfortunately, there are no current standard definitions of these greater urban areas and thus they will need to be defined spatially. Nonetheless, there is no doubt that there has been both an expansion of the major urban centres as well as densification of the land uses within these centres.

From casual observation, sections of the Southern Main Road in Chaguanas between Montrose and the town centre; the Eastern Main Road between Laventille to Trincity; and Milford Road between Canaan and Bon Accord have shown both infilling of vacant lots as well as increase in height and bulk of buildings. The construction of new shopping malls and the expansion of the older ones have been pursued in North, Central and South Trinidad, and in Tobago. Within the last five years or so, there has been an increase in physical development in all of the major urban centres of both Trinidad and Tobago. A large number of green field sites have been used for new housing subdivisions, industrial estates, sporting complexes, commercial establishments, health centres, schools and roads. The demand for land for built development is pushed, inter alia, by an expanding population with higher expectations, and a government with a commitment to the provision of adequate housing and gas-based industrialisation. 4.2.2 Pressure The tremendous increase in physical development has expanded and densified urban centres with increasing space devoted to utilities, transportation, housing, industry and commerce. The escalating demand has given rise to land clearance, building on steeper and higher slopes, land reclamation and the provision of a range of services and amenities. These actions have led to impacts on the land such as clearing of green field sites, modification of the land form, changes in natural drainage patterns, siltation of water courses, flooding and traffic jams. In the marine environment, reclamation works are destroying mangrove vegetation, feeding grounds and nurseries for fish and other marine life forms. Housing estates are being developed on some 10 sites from Caroni’s ex-sugar cane fields. According to the Estate Management Business Development Company (EMBD), some 1,375 ha of land have been identified for residential subdivision (Table 4.3). The lot yield is still not known but the population of these areas will increase upon occupation of these buildings. Some 34 housing estates are being developed by the National Housing Authority (NHA)/Housing Development Corporation (HDC) (Table 4.4) throughout the country on green field sites, some of which were until quite recently, productive sugar cane lands owned by Caroni (1975) Limited.
