The country’s per capita rating is expected to climb with the expansion of its industrialisation process and the ever-increasing vehicle population. The concerted international response to stratospheric ozone depletion has controlled the discharge of ozone-depleting substances (ODS), and as a result the ‘ozone hole’ observed annually in Antarctica has started to shrink and is predicted to close by 2050. On the home front, Trinidad and Tobago has done its share to reduce the use of ODS, and more efforts are expected. Outdoor air quality issues, such as vehicular emissions and particulate pollutants are examined based on available data, and the information points to the fact that these are worrying concerns. One of the main sources of air pollution, which negatively affects outdoor air quality, is motor vehicles, which is also a key contributor to GHG emissions. Industrial activities and bushfires are major contributors to particulate pollution. Though there are plans for continuous measurement of air pollutant levels, monitoring is mostly confined to individual isolated studies. Public awareness about the health (as well as other) effects of air pollution and ‘air toxics’, is important in ensuring control of the problem. Sahara dust is increasingly becoming a threat which poses health concerns for those affected by asthma and other respiratory ailments. An ongoing research programme has been established at the St Augustine Campus of the University of the West Indies (UWI) to examine the composition and impacts of Sahara Dust in Trinidad. There are a number of emerging issues such as indoor air quality, exposure to cigarette smoke, etc. for which there is limited information but which may need future attention. 3.2 Climate Variability and Change 3.2.1 Background Weather refers to the daily and weekly changes in the atmosphere experienced in a given location as it relates to rainfall, temperature, air pressure, relative humidity, cloudiness, wind, and visibility. On the other hand, climate refers to the long term description of weather patterns. Historically, a 30-year period has been taken to represent the long-term or normal climate of a country. The current standard 30-year climatology as defined by the World Meteorological Organisation (WMO) to assess changes in climate is from 1961 to 1990. Both short term weather patterns and longer term climate can be variable. Climate variability can be defined as the way climatic variables, such as temperature and precipitation, depart from some average state, either above or below the average value. Climate change, on the other hand, can be defined as a trend in one or more climatic variables characterised by a fairly smooth continuous increase or decrease of the average value during the period of record. While there is continued debate about what constitutes normal climate variability as opposed to actual climate change, only a few will argue that current global climatic trends are not a worrying concern. There is also the incidence of extreme weather events that depart from the average in some significant way. Global climate change is expected to result in an increase in the occurrence of these extreme events.
