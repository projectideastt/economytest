The Case of Diesel Engines Diesel engines are generally more efficient, which allows them to emit a significantly lower concentration of carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons than equivalent gasoline engines. However, diesel engines are a significant source of nitrogen oxides, specifically nitric oxide, which reacts photo-chemically with hydrocarbons to form ground level ozone or smog. Data from the recently commissioned ambient air monitoring equipment at Point Lisas confirms that ground level ozone is formed and peaks at around the same time as peak traffic hours. Additionally, diesel engines emit a significant amount of particulate matter in exhaust gases, particularly in urban areas and high traffic areas. Under the same engine and load conditions, diesel engines emit 100 times more particulates than gasoline engines. Although diesel exhaust contains over 450 different chemical components, the main threats of diesel emissions to public health can be classified under three broad categories: fine particulate matter, oxides of nitrogen, and toxic compounds. One such is the Draft Motor Vehicle and Road Traffic (Air Pollution) Regulations which targets vehicles emitting ‘smoke’ and without necessary ‘equipment/device’ that would minimise the release of air pollutants. Under these proposed regulations there would be penalties for violators and vehicles are expected to be inspected and certified for its road worthiness. To date, it is only under Rule 38 (13) of the Motor Vehicles and Road Traffic Regulations, made under Section 100 of the Motor Vehicles and Road Traffic Act, that persons emitting smoke, visible vapours, sparks, or oils, can be prosecuted.
