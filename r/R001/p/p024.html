It also offers ecosystems which encourage the development of biodiversity, including a refuge for migratory species of birds, and ensures appropriate sites for the impoundment of surface water run-off. The three issues dealt with in respect of inland waters are surface water availability and quantity, ground water availability and quality, and aquatic ecosystems-wetland health. Human action has generated pressure on the resource. Surface and ground water resources and wetlands receive a range of pollutants from agriculture (agro-chemicals), industry (chemicals including some of the heavy metals), faecal coliform (raw sewage from malfunctioning treatment plants) and solid and hazardous waste from urban areas and particularly residential commercial activities. Wetlands are impacted by all of the above, but are further stressed by land reclamation and physical development especially port, industrial activities and resort/residential development. As a consequence of the pressure, there are some changes in the resource. Abstraction and diversion have resulted in reduced volume of water in our rivers, while increased sedimentation has reduced their widths and depths. Other changes include a decrease in the quality of both surface and ground water; increasing cost of water production; and reduction in size and quality of water recharge areas. The country has sustained more than 50 % loss of wetlands over the years and wetland loss continues as corporate and individual developers transform the wetland resources into physical development. Responses have been at both the national and international levels. At the national level the Water Pollution Rules will make continued practices of pollution much more difficult and costly for the polluters. The National Reforestation and Watershed Rehabilitation Programme which is intended to replant some 33,000 hectares of denuded land over a 10-year period is being implemented, and a National Wetlands Committee prepared a National Wetlands Policy which was accepted by the Government. At the international level, the Government acceded to the Convention for Wetlands of International Importance. Biodiversity Trinidad and Tobago has a rich variety of biological resources, given its geological relationship to the South American mainland. Although the review of taxa in Trinidad and Tobago is far from complete, at latest count the fauna for the archipelago exceeds 1,000 species and the flora, 2,500 species. A number of challenges face Trinidad and Tobago in its task to maintain species biodiversity and to conserve the country’s wildlife habitats. The chapter on Biodiversity looks at the issues of habitat conservation and protection, species protection, and biodiversity and climate change. The major pressures on habitat conservation and protection are loss, fragmentation and degradation - due mainly to deforestation, expansion of human settlement and continued urbanisation, forest degradation, forest fires, pollution, and the physical alteration of habitat - all of which ultimately affect species’ numbers in terrestrial, riverine and marine environments. The chapter looks at forest degradation and loss, and decreasing water quantity and quality as major pressures acting on the environment. In an effort to conserve and protect the country’s biodiversity, there are some 36 forest reserves, 13 wildlife sanctuaries, 1 national park, 3 beaches where entry is restricted seasonally (i.e., during the sea turtle nesting season) and 1 marine protected area. However the proportion of ecosystems represented in the various protected areas is not known. The reforestation of degraded forests is being undertaken as part of the National Reforestation and Watershed Rehabilitation Programme (NRWRP). Between 2003 and 2005, 1,825 hectares of forest lands have been replanted and maintained and an additional 14,042 hectares of forest protected.
